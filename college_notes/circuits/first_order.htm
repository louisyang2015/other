Transient Analysis of First Order Circuits

First Order Circuits --- a circuit with one capacitor or one inductor. The current and voltage for these circuits are described by a differential equation of the form:

dt
+ a*x(t) = f(t)

If f(t) is a constant source, the solution will take the form:

x(t) = k1 + k2 e-t/T

k1 is called the steady state solution since:

T --- is the time constant of the circuit.

The K2 e-t/T term is roughly zero after 4 time constants.

Another property of T is that the initial slope of the curve y = K2 e-t/T will intersect the horizon axis at T. Example, the graph of: y = e-t/1

Inductor and Capacitor Behavior

Inductors will try to hold the current constant.

The differential equation for an inductor is:

v(t) = L *
d i(t)

This equation produces a v(t) --- so it's best to use loop analysis with inductor circuits.

At switching time, inductors will act as current sources.

Capacitors will try to hold the voltage constant.

The differential equation for a capacitor is:

i(t) = C *
d v(t)
dt

This equation produces a i(t) --- so it's best to use nodal analysis with capacitor circuits.

At switching time, capacitors will act as voltage sources.

A way to avoid confusing one differential equation with another is to first recognize the pattern:

For capacitors, that blue box is C. For inductors that blue box is L.

The yellow box is whatever the device attempts holds constant at switching time. Capacitors hold voltage constant, so the yellow box for capacitor is v(t). Inductors hold current constant, so the yellow box is i(t) for inductors.

Solving First Order Differential Equations - Finding K1, K2

The solution to the first homogeneous order differential equation is:

x(t) = k1 + k2 e-t/T

To get k1, k2, you would have to compute some kind of initial condition or steady state condition. You need to use circuit analysis to find out x(∞), and that allows you to get k1. Then you use circuit analysis to get x(0) or x'(0) to get k2 .

Two Options for Finding the Time Constant (T)

The standard way is to set up the differential equation, then find root for the characteristic equation, and get the T.

An alternative way to get the T make use of these two results:

In a circuit with a single capacitor, T = RC, where R is the Thevenin resistance from the viewpoint of the capacitor.

In a circuit with a single inductor, T = L/R, where R is the Thevenin resistance from the viewpoint of the inductor.

With first order circuits, you don't have to set up differential equations. You can just compute the Thevenin resistance and then compute the T.

The three constants will be solved in the following order: K1, K2, T

With inductor first order circuits, the procedure is the same except that at t = 0, the inductor is trying to maintain a specific current level and so is acting as a current source.

Switching Action at t = t0

In the previous example, the switching action occurs at time t = 0. If the switch occurs at another time, say time t = t0, the solution will be shifted to t0 units. The format of this shifted solution is:

x(t) = K1 + K2 e-(t-t0)/T
