Scaling

If you have a known circuit and want to only modify a few things, then you might not have to redesign that whole circuit. Much of the following is common sense.

Magnitude Scaling:

If you want the impedance over a resistor to be twice as big, use a resistor twice as big.

If you want the impedance over an inductor to be twice as big, use an inductor twice as big.

If you want the impedance over a capacitor to be twice as big, use an capacitor twice as small.

For the series RLC and parallel RLC circuits, scaling all impedances by the same constant (K) does not change the circuit's resonance frequency, quality, and bandwidth.

Frequency Scaling:

Say you already have a circuit and its resonance frequency is known to be w0 . You would like to modify the circuit to produce resonance at a new frequency K*w0 . What you need to do is to modify the components so that at the new frequency K*w0 the components produce the same impedance as they use to produce at the old frequency w0 .

A resistor produces the same impedance at all frequencies. No modification will be done to resistors.

The impedance of the old inductor Lold at w0 = the impedance of the new inductor ( Lold / K ) at the frequency ( K * w0 )

The impedance of the old capacitor Cold at w0 = the impedance of the new capacitor ( Cold / K ) at the frequency ( K * w0 )

Note: if you replace Lold with Lold / K, and replace Cold with Cold / K, then the resonance frequency will be moved from w0 to K * w0 . However, the quality factor is unchanged, so the bandwidth is enlarged: BWnew = K * BWold .
