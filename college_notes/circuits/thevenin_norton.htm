Thevenin's and Norton's Theorems

These two theorems are about dividing a circuit into two linear pieces.

Since both circuits are linear, the actual value of "i" and "Vo" can be determined as the superposition of the effects from circuit A and circuit B.

From the view point of circuit B, circuit A can be reduced to a form with a voltage source in series wit a resistor. This is the Thevenin form:

Voc is the open circuit voltage of circuit A

And Rth is the equivalent resistance you get by looking into circuit A.

To get circuit A into the Thevenin form, you need to comput Voc and Rth

The Norton form is to replace circuit A with a current source and a resistor:

isc is the short circuit current of circuit A

And Rth is the same as before --- the resistance obtained by looking into the terminals of circuit A

To get circuit A into Norton form you need to compute isc and Rth.

Rth Calculation

For both the Norton form and the Thevenin form, you need this Rth. There are several scenarios for calculating the Rth.

If circuit A has only independent sources, Rth is obtained by looking into circuit A and setting all voltage and current sources to zero --- a voltage source with value zero is a short circuit while a current source with value zero is a open circuit.

If circuit A has only dependent sources, determine Rth by applying a test charge at circuit A's terminals.
