AC Steady-State Analysis - Phasors

AC steady state analysis is a more generalized version of DC steady state analysis. The current, voltage, and resistance are all complex numbers. Note: resistance in complex number is called impedance.

How to use phasors

If all the independent sources in an AC circuit take the form:

	something * cos(100t + something)

then all of the the current and voltages in the circuit will also take the form:

	something * cos(100t + something)

When we solve these AC steady state circuits, the type of function (sine or cosine), and the frequency of the function (100t), is the same throughout the circuit. The amplitude and the phase is what differs for each component in the circuit.

The phasor is a complex number in polar form that tracks the amplitude and phase shift.

Say we have a voltage source that is: 2 30o V. This means that the voltage source is sinusoidal, with an amplitude of 2 and a phase shift of 30 degrees. The voltage source could be any of the following:

  • 2 * sin(wt + 30o)
  • 2 * cos(wt + 30o)

We do not know what frequency the voltage source is at, nor do we know we know if the voltage source is sine or cosine.

2 30o V = 1.7321 + j V.

In this manner, voltages and currents are complex numbers.

The "resistance" of inductors and capacitors are also complex numbers.

Inductor's "resistance" = Lwj
Capacitor's "resistance" = 1/(Cwj)

A "resistance" that is a complex number is called impedance.

Why Phasors Work

It's a long sequence of events:

A  Φ --> A ejΦ --> A ej(wt + Φ) 
--> A cos(wt + Φ) + j * A sin(wt + Φ) 
--> take real or imaginary part as answer

A Φ --> A ejΦ

A Φ is a notation for the complex number A ejΦ

A ejΦ --> A ej(wt + Φ)

The wt was dropped out because it was everywhere. Example:

I1 = 2 ej(wt + 20)
I2 = 3 ej(wt + 30)

I1 + I2 = ejwt (2 e20j + 3 e30j)

There's a new amplitude and new phase --- but ejwt is factored out and unaffected by circuit analysis.

The A ej(wt + Φ)  form is used to derive the impedance formulas for capacitor and inductor.

For example, the inductor formula is v(t) = L * [d i(t)/dt]

If we let i(t) = A ej(wt + Φ) , then we get v(t) = L * w * A ej(wt + Φ)

So the v(t) / i(t) ratio is Lwj

The same applies for the capacitor.

A ej(wt + Φ) --> A cos(wt + Φ) + j * A sin(wt + Φ)

This is just an expansion from the polar form to the rectangular form.

A cos(wt + Φ) + j * A sin(wt + Φ) --> take real or imaginary part as answer

This relates to the solution method of a differential equation (see ac_steady_part1.htm).

When I previously said i(t) = A ej(wt + Φ) , I never meant it literally. i(t) is really the real or imaginary part of A ej(wt + Φ) . We use e(...) instead of sin(...) or cos(...) for the ease of calculation offered by the complex numbers.
