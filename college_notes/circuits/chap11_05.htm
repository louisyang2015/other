Filters

Filters are divided into two groups. Passive filters use only capacitors and inductors to achieve filtering. Active filters use amplifiers in addition to capacitors and inductors.

Passive Filters

The voltage drop across a capacitor will be high if the frequency is low --- so taking the output across a capacitor is always going to result in a low pass filter.

The voltage drop across an inductor will be high if the frequency is high --- so taking the output across an inductor is always going to result in a high pass filter.

Active Filters

Passive circuits can not really have a gain > 1. While it is true that in the series RLC circuit, the output voltage across the capacitor can be quite high, putting almost any load across the capacitor will change things drastically. The reason is that any load will allow the current to walk around the capacitor.

Active filters use amplifiers in addition to capacitors and inductors. It's the amplifier that will enable the gain to be greater than one, while the capacitor and inductors are the components doing the filtering.

This section involve two different kinds of amplifiers --- the ideal op-amp that is from section 3.3, and also a new operational transconductance amplifier (OTA).

In the OTA, the output is a current that is a multiple of the input voltage.

For both the ideal op-amp and ideal OTA, the goal is to have the amplifier work no matter what components are attached at the input and at the output.

For the op-amp, ideal Rout is 0 because the ideal op-amp is viewed as a voltage provider. The ideal OTA has an ideal Rout of 0 because it's suppose to be a current provider.

OTA can be configured to act as a resistor. The reason that this is done is that OTA can be made of transistors, and transistors takes up much less space than a resistor.
