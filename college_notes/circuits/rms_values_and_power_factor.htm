The rms values are scalar values obtained through the root-mean-square procedure. Using the rms value, the power absorbed by a resistor is always:

P = |Irms| * |Vrms| , regardless of the wave form.

Rms values for sinusoidal waves:

√2 |Irms| = |Imax|

√2 |Vrms| = |Vmax|

The rms value is a replacement for the amplitude value of waves. It's like a new unit system. One example is that the standard AC electrical outlet at home have an rms value of 120 volt and a maximum value of 120*√2 V , which is 169.71V.

My convention and the book's convention:

If there is ever confusion of a letter being an amplitude or a phasor, my convention is that |Irms| is the amplitude of the phasor Irms . So the root-mean-square procedure obtains an amplitude. If we use that amplitude to describe a phasor, the phasor is called Irms .

The book will use italics to denote amplitude, and bold value to denote the phasor --- which I do not think is a good policy.

Power Factor (pf)

pf = cos(θv - θi)

The usual case is that a load is an RL load, so the current lags the voltage. We say that this load has a lagging pf , and θv - θi is positive.

The other case is that a load is an RC load, so the voltage lags the current. We say that this load has a leading pf , and θv - θi is negative.

It is economically advantageous to have a power factor (pf) as close to one as possible.

In the extreme case that the power factor is zero, then all of the power plant's output is lost over the transmission lines.

A higher power factor means that the plant will not have to work as hard to satisfy the load.
