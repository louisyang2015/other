Linearity and Superposition

Linearity

"Linear elements" include resistors, capacitors, linearly dependent sources. I am not sure what the criteria is --- but maybe when the Laplace transform is done on a function relating voltage and current, that function needs to be linear. Oh just accept it that resistors, capacitors, and linearly dependent sources are linear elements, and if a circuit is made of linear elements, then it's linear.

If the circuit is linear, with a single source, and I am measuring the voltage across a particular resistor, then doubling that single source will also double the voltage across that resistor. The same idea applies to the current of the resistor --- double the source, get double current. This relationship is called "linearity" of the circuit.

The linearity thing only works for voltages and currents --- maybe the criteria is that you do the Laplace transform on the equations involved, and the result is a linear transfer function.

Power is not a linear quantity --- so if you double the source, you won't get double the power. (twice current times twice voltage gives four times the power).

A part of the book actually uses linearity to solve problems --- you basically assume the input voltage is ONE volt, and you solve the circuit say for the output voltage. Then the input voltage is really TWO volts, so you double the output voltage that you previously computed as use that as the answer. --- it's of course a rather silly way to solve problems --- the book just wants to show case the concept, that's all.

Superposition

If the circuit is linear, and you are computing a linear quantity, you can consider each source individually and add up their contributions. Say you want the voltage across a resistor and the circuit has two voltage sources: A and B.

You can set B to zero, and solve the circuit to see how much A contributes to the voltage across the resistor. Then you set A to zero, and solve the circuit to see how much B  contributes to the voltage across the resistor. Finally you add the two contributions up to determine the final answer. It's like that linearity thing --- an important circuit concept, but much less important in terms of problem solving.

To set a voltage source to zero is the same as replacing it with a short circuit. To set a current source to zero is the same as replacing it with an open circuit.
