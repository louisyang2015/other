The higher the Q is, the sharper the peak of the transfer function becomes, and the more selective the filter becomes.

For a series circuit, the magnitude of the voltage across the capacitor and inductor (VC and VL) under resonance conditions is

|VC| = Q * |V1|

|VL| = Q * |V1|

When Q is high, the voltage across the capacitor (VC) can be so high that the capacitor becomes damaged.

 

Energy Dissipation --- A more General Definition of Q

Q = 2π *
maximum energy stored at resonance
energy dissipated per cycle

We can go from this definition of Q to the equations that we already have.

Maximum energy stored at resonance:

In the LRC circuit, during one half of a cycle the inductor is storing energy while the capacitor is losing energy. During the following half cycle, the inductor is losing energy while the capacitor is storing energy. So the maximum energy stored at resonance = the maximum energy stored by inductor = the maximum energy stored by the capacitor.

From chapter 5, the equations for energy:

w(t) = 0.5 * C * v2(t)
w(t) = 0.5 * L * i2(t)

The equation for the capacitor uses the voltage over the capacitor. If we use that equation, the maximum energy stored is: 0.5 * C * (Q * |V1|)2

If we use the inductor equation, the maximum energy stored is 0.5 * L * | I |2

Energy dissipated per cycle:

The average power over the resistor: | I | * | V1 | * 0.5

The duration of the cycle, under resonance conditions, is: (2Π) / w0

The energy dissipated per cycle: [ (2Π) / w0 ] * ( | I | * | V1 | * 0.5 )

1 + 2ST * (jw) + (jw * T)2

In terms of the Bode magnitude plot, a quadratic term in the denominator means that we are bending downward 40 dB per decade. So if we are previously moving upward at 20 dB per decade, it is the quadratic term makes the circuit a band pass filter.
