Registers

Segment Registers

There are four segment registers:

  • code segment --- CS
  • data segment --- DS
  • stack segment --- SS
  • extra segment --- ES

the "extra segment" ES register don't have a particular job --- it's just a segment for your own use. For 386 and beyond, there's also FS, GS --- two more segment registers

 

General Purpose Registers

32 bit registers: EAX, EBX, ECX, EDX, EBP, ESI, EDI, and ESP

The name of the lower 16 bits regions within the above registers: AX, BX, CX, DX, BP, SI, DI, SP (SP is a little less general than the others)

The key idea is that AX is the lower 16 bit of EAX --- AX is not a distinct register

The AX, BX, CX, DX registers are in turn divided into two halves, each 8 bit: AH, AL, BH, BL, and so on

 

IP and FLAGS register

You do not deal directly with these two registers as a unit --- they are there and they are dealt with indirectly through instruction like: jump, check overflow, etc.

IP = instruction pointer

Your program can have many code segments --- and CS stores the code segment that you are in at a particular time. IP is the instruction pointer, and CS:IP defines where you are in the execution. You don't load stuff into the IP register directly --- you use the JMP instruction.
