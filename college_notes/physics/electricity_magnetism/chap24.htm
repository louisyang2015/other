Gauss's Law

Electric Flux

Electric flux is represented by the number of electric field lines penetrating some surface.

Φ = E * A

Φ = electric flux
E = electric field that is orthogonal to area A

If the area (A) is not orthogonal to the electric field lines, then we use an area (A cos θ) that is orthogonal to the electric field lines.

So the more general formula for flux is

Φ = E * A cos(θ)

If E and A are expressed as vectors, the above is a dot product of E and A. The most general expression for flux is:

Φ = ∫ E dA

where E dA is a dot product and the integral over dA is a surface integral

Charge External to a Closed Surface

If we have a charge external to a closed surface, the number of flux lines entering the surface equals the number of flux lines leaving it. So a charge external to a closed surface results in a net flux of zero.

The net electric flux through any closed surface is equal to the net charge inside the surface divided by ε0 .

The key to using Gauss's law in the book's problems is to pick a surface where the electric field E is constant throughout the surface. In that case, we can factor the E out of the integral and write:

Also, if E is orthogonal to the surface area, then that dot product will default to multiplication.

Conductors in Electrostatic Equilibrium

Conductors in electrostatic equilibrium means that there are carriers in the conductor, but there is no current.

Since there is no current, there is no electric field in the conductor.

The

Φ = ∫ E dA =
qin
ε0

expression then implies that when the electric field is zero, the charge is zero. If a solid conductor does have charge, all of the charge must be at the surface of the conductor.

Another result of Gauss's law is that the electric field just outside a charged conductor is perpendicular to the surface of the conductor and has a magnitude of p / ε0 , where p is the charge per unit area at that point.

On a irregularly shaped conductor, charge tends to accumulate at locations where the radius of curvature of the surface is the smallest, that is, at sharp points.
