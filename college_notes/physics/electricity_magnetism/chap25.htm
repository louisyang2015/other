Charged Conductors

For a charged conductor, the charges all move the surface. The surface of a charged conductor becomes an equipotential surface --- so that charges on the surface will stop moving.

The inside of a conductor has an electric field of zero, so the voltage stays constants throughout the conductor --- which again stops charges from moving.

For a nonspherical conductor, the charge density is highest at the sharp corners. At those places, electric field can become so high that it ionizes the surrounding air and make that surrounding air conduct. This is called corona discharged. Air by default has a small amount of ions to begin with. In corona discharge, the electric field is so high that the few ions in air are made to move so rapidly that they collide with other air molecules to create even more ion in the process.

This means by having a shell that acts as a conductor, a circuit or even an entire building can be shielded from external fields.

 

Experiments and Applications

Millikan Oil-Drop Experiment

An electric field is used to affect how fast drops of oil fall. The experiment found the magnitude of the elementary charge.

Van de Graaff Generator

When you apply charge to the cavity of a conductor, all that charge will move to the surface of the conductor. What a Van de Graaff generator does is that it keeps applying charges to the cavity of a conductor, eventually causing a huge build up of charge and voltage on the surface of the conductor.

Electrostatic Precipitator

If a sufficient electric field is applied to polluted air, the dirt in the air is ionized. The ionized dirt is then filtered out through electrostatic attraction/repulsion, and the polluted air is cleaned.

Xerography

This refers to the technology used by the copy machines to copy documents. This process uses a photoconductor, which is a poor conductor in the absence of light and a good conductor in the presence of light. By shining light on paper and by observing how much light is reflected, the copy machine can tell if a section of a paper is black or white. The relationship to electrostatics is that a charged powder is sprayed on the photoconductor before the copying process. During the copy process, sections of the photoconductor that has be exposed to stronger light will conduct away more of the charged powder. So the layer of powder becomes an electrostatic image of the paper, where the higher charged areas corresponds to the darker areas of the page.

Field-Ion Microscope

Here, a metallic element with a very sharp corner develops a very high electric field at the corner. A helium atom wondering nearby will lose the electron to the metallic element, making the helium itself charged. The charged helium gets sent to a fluorescent screen, and the resulting pattern of helium on the screen represents an image of the metallic object's sharp corner.
