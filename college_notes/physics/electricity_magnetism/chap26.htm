Combinations of Capacitors

For capacitors in parallel, Qtotal = Q1 + Q2 , and so Ctotal = C1 + C2 .

For capacitors in series, just like any other component in series, Vtotal = V1 + V2 , and so (1 / Ctotal) = (1/C1) + (1/C2).

The dielectric strength is the maximum electric field strength that can exist without having an electric discharge from one charged plate to the other. If we call this number Emax , then the maximum charge that can be stored on a parallel plate capacitor is: Qmax = C * Vmax , and Vmax = Emax * d .

Some Capacitor designs:

In the electrolyte capacitor, a fluid fills the capacitor. The fluid itself is conductive. If the voltage across the capacitor is applied as specified, the liquid forms a thin layer of metal oxide (an insulator) on the foil, and this layer serves as the dielectric. If the polarity of the applied voltage is opposite what is intended, the oxide layer is removed and the capacitor conducts electricity rather than stores charge.

Some molecules, like water, are permanently polarized, meaning they have a positive end and a negative end. Other molecules can be polarized under the influence of an external electric field. In these cases, the molecules line up when an electric field is applied:

In a capacitor, the lining up of molecules according to the electric field introduces additional charges just inside the capacitor plates. These charges attract additional charges to stick on the capacitor plates, increasing the capacitance. This is how dielectric materials work.

In the picture above, the (σ) is suppose to be charge density.

Another way to describe what is going on is to say that the charges between the plates are creating their own electric field (Ei) that weakens the electric field that would be created in the absence of the dielectric material (E0).

So in the absence of a dielectric material, E = E0 = σi / ε0

With a dielectric material inserted, E = E0 - Ei , and this E = E0 / κ .
