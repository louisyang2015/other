The Magnetic Field

There are two sources of magnetic fields. One is natural magnets, the other is a moving charge.

The direction of the magnetic field lines:

Note that the north pole of the compass actually points to a south pole.

Because a moving charge generates a magnetic field, it acts as a magnet. If the moving charge is going through an external magnetic field, it will experience a force.

F = qv × B

F = force felt
q = charge; remember to use negative value for electrons
v = velocity of charge
B = external magnetic field

A comparison of electric (E) and magnetic (B) fields

  • E is in the direction of force while B is perpendicular to the force
  • B gets created only if the charge is moving, E is created even if the charge is at rest
  • B does no work because it's force is perpendicular to motion
  • That last point also means that B can alter direction, but not displacement - so B doesn't change a particle's kinetic energy

 

Magnetic Force on a Current Carrying Conductor

dF = I ds × B

For a curved wire:

dF = I * L' × B

where L is a vector from one end point to the other. This allows us to ignore the curves.

For a current loop,

total F = 0.

 

Torque on a Current Loop in a Uniform Magnetic Field

A conducting wire loop is placed in the mist of a magnetic field.

The side (a) will not experience any force, but side (b) will experience a force that cause the wire loop to rotate.

When the loop is tilted

F1 = F2 = IbB

torque (τ) = F1 (a/2) sin θ + F2 (a/2) sin θ

τ = μ × B

where

μ = I * A

and the μ is called the magnetic moment of the current loop.

A is a vector that points upward from the current loop. The magnitude of A is the area of the loop.

The τ = μ × B result in this section are quite general. Any orientation of B will work, and any loop shape will work.

Motion of a Charged Particle in a Magnetic Field

A moving charge experiences a magnetic force that is perpendicular to the direction of motion --- gosh, this sounds just like centripetal acceleration. Actually, a charge does go in circles in a magnetic field.

In the above analysis, "v" is perpendicular to a constant B. If "v" is not perpendicular to B, then the component of "v" that is parallel to B will move to the particle in a helix pattern.

If the B field is not constant, the movement of the particle can become quite complicated. Such non-uniform "magnetic bottles" are the key to containing plasma and creating fusion.

Lorentz force --- this is when the charge moves in a B field and an E field at the same time, so the charge experience an electric force in addition to the magnetic force.

F = qE + qv × B

Hall Effect --- The hall effect invovles the the Lorentz force concept. A conductor is placed in a magnetic field, and a voltage is induced in a direction perpendicular to both the current and the magnetic field.

The charges will be deflected to one side of the conductor as a result of the qvB force, which then creates an electric field to counter the qvB force. At steady state, the qvB force is exactly balanced by an internal electric field so that the charge build up on one side of the conductor will not go on forever.

qvB = qEH reduces to:
vB = EH

VH = EH * d

where VH is the Hall voltage.
