This "ε" means "electromotive force", which is the same thing as voltage.

The "ΦB" is magnetic flux:

The original Faraday's experiment:

When the switch is flipped on or off, there is a change in the magnetic field of the primary coil, resulting in a short term current in the circuit on the right side, and the galvanometer needle is deflected momentarily. We say that "an induced emf is produced in the secondary circuit by the changing magnetic field."

A Conductor Moving in a Magnetic Field

In this case, a charge q in the magnetic field would experience force: qvB. The charges have no where to go, and so the force qvB acting on a charge must be balanced by an internal electric field of the same magnitude.

Force per charge = vB

Voltage across the conductor = ℓ * vB

A Growing Loop in a Magnetic Field

In this case, force Fapp is moving the green bar to the right and increasing the area of the conductor loop. The flux is therefore increasing, and a voltage is induced that will generate a current.

The current "I" will interact with the magnetic field to create a force Fm that will work against the Fapp . It has to be this way because if Fm is in the direction of Fapp , then the smallest push Fapp will send the bar accelerating forever.

The power that it takes to pull the green bar to the right is also the power of the circuit. Power = Fapp * velocity = (Voltage)2 / R .

Lenz's Law --- The polarity of the induced emf is such that it tends to produce a current that will create a magnetic flux to oppose the change in magnetic flux through the loop.

In the above example, we move a magnet such that the field lines are pushed one way into a conductor loop, and a counter flux is created such that the field lines move the opposite way through the loop.

Generators and Motors

A generator can be created by rotating a conductor loop in a magnetic field.

For practical purposes there are usually N loops rotating in a magnetic field. A voltage will be generated across each loop according to Faraday's law. The variation of the magnetic flux is:

To have a more steady output, have loops that are out of phase, and then add these out of phase signals together.

In a motor, the current being fed is varied so that the conducting loop will rotate in a magnetic field. As the conductor loop rotates, it will generate its own magnetic flux that counters the external magnetic field. This counter magnetic flux will reduce the voltage across the conductor loop. The voltage reduction is called back emf.

As an example, say a motor has coils of 10 Ω, supplied by a voltage of 120 V, and the back emf is 70V. Initially, the coils are not rotating so to turn on the motor, a current of 120 V / 10 Ω = 12 A is needed. After the motor is turned on, the rotating coils will induced a 70 V that counters the 120 V supplied. The running motor will only require (120 V - 70 V) / 10 Ω = 5 A current. Many motors are like this --- that the current to keep the motor running is far less than the current to start the motor.

Eddy Currents

Eddy currents can be generated when you swing a piece of metal in between two magnetic poles.

As the metal enters, the ΔΦ is into the paper. As the metal leaves, the ΔΦ is out of the paper. In both cases, there's a drag force Fm. This drag force is used to slow trains in some subways - the electromagnets are mounted on the rail.

To stop eddy currents, we need to make parts of the metal non-conductive. For example, cutting pieces of it out would help.

In real life eddy currents are undesirable because they cause energy lose in the form of heat. So usually, moving conducting parts are laminated - they are build up in thin layers separated by insulators.
