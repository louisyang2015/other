Fundamentals of Thermal Radiation

Introduction

Conduction and convection can not happen in a vacuum, but radiation can.

Radiation transfer can happen between two bodies that are separated by a medium colder than both bodies.

All object above absolute zero sends out thermal radiation, and the bulk of this radiation is over the 0.1 to 100 um range. This range includes infrared light, visible light, and some ultraviolet light.

Almost half of solar radiation is light --- it falls into the visible light range.

In a solid, the molecules throughout the solid is vibrating --- hence radiation is really a volumetric phenomenon. But radiation from the interior of the solid doesn't really get out and is instead reabsorbed, so we treat radiation as a surface phenomenon.

Blackbody Concepts

A blackbody is a perfect emitter and absorber of radiation. This sounds contradictory at first, but remember that reflection is not the same as emission.

A blackbody emits radiation uniformly in all directions --- this quality is called diffusive.

A blackbody is not the same as a visually black object. Any object that absorbs the visible range of thermal radiation will appear black. A true blackbody will absorb thermal radiation overall the entire range of the spectrum.

A large cavity with a small opening resembles a blackbody.

The object in the above picture can be treated as a blackbody with surface area A and temperature T.

Most radiation rays that enter the small opening will require multiple reflections to leave the same small opening. Conversely, radiation rays emanating from the cavity surface will go through multiple reflections before reaching the small opening, so that rays reaching the opening will be diffusive.

Assume E is a constant value throughout the patch of area dA. Then assume we have diffusive radiation. In that case, the intensity I would be constant throughout the hemisphere. The relationship between E and I:

E = π I

The solid angle containing the entire hemisphere is 2π, but I does not equal E / (2π) even though I is E per steradian. The reason is that for any particular point on the hemisphere, only E * cos θ is aimed at a particular point on the hemisphere.

The E = π I thing works in reverse too. We can have radiation going through the hemisphere striking the patch of area dA from all directions. This is called irradiation (G), which is the same as E except that G is striking dA while E is leaving dA. The radiation intensity (I) on the hemisphere would be G = π * I.

If dA is a patch of blackbody, then there is no reflection and E = π * I.

If dA is not blackbody, the radiation leaving dA includes both a reflection component and an emission component. Eemitted = π Ie , Ereflected = π Ir , Etotal = Eemitted + Ereflected .

Absorptivity (α) = fraction of radiation absorbed by the surface

Reflectivity (ρ) = fraction of radiation reflected by the surface

Transmissivity (τ) = fraction of radiation transmitted by the surface

Opaque surfaces have τ = 0, α + ρ = 1.

αλ , ρλ , τλ --- are property values at particular wave lengths.

If the temperature of an object is not changing, then α = ε because what is absorbed should be emitted. We can also say αλ = ελ

The absorption and emission can happen at different wave lengths. This is what happens in a car with windows closed. The sun light goes through the glass windows and get absorbed by the inside of the car. The inside of the car then emits heat as infrared wave --- due to the cooler temperature of the inside of the car as opposed to the sun. The glass is in turn opaque to the infrared wave, and hence traps the heat inside the car. This is known as the greenhouse effect. At night the earth also emits heat to outer space in the form of infrared radiation. Certain elements of our atmosphere, like CO2 and water vapor, lets the sun light thorough but blocks the exiting infrared radiation like the glass in a car. Areas without CO2 and water vapor would experience larger swing between daytime and nighttime temperatures.

Diffusive and Gray Surfaces

Diffusive surfaces have the properties (ε , α , ρ , τ) independent of direction (θ).

Gray surfaces have the properties (ε , α , ρ , τ) independent of wavelength (λ).

Surfaces that are both diffusive and gray will have the properties (ε , α , ρ , τ) independent of direction (θ) and wavelength (λ).

Real surfaces come close to emitting radiation in a diffusive manner.

The properties (ε , α , ρ , τ) are strongly dependent on surface condition such as oxidation, roughness, type of finish, and cleanliness.

Atmospheric and Solar Radiation

The solar energy striking earth: 1373 W/m2 .

This is the solar energy incident on a surface normal to the sun's rays at the outer edge of the atmosphere when the earth is at its mean distance from the sun.

Parts of the atmosphere will absorb some of this energy, and the actual energy that reach Earth's surface is about 950 W/m2 on a clear day and much less on cloudy or smoggy days.

We can estimate the sun's surface temperature using the idea that the heat that leaves a spherical shell representing the sun will also go through a spherical shell containing earth's orbit around the sun, and by assuming that the sun is a blackbody.

(4π L2) 1373 = (4π r2) σ T4

L = distance between earth and sun
r = radius of the sun

The ozone absorbs harmful ultra violet light. Dust in the atmosphere scatters certain color of light, giving the sky its color.

The scattered light will eventually reach earth's surface uniformly from all directions --- diffuse solar radiation Gd. The light that did not get scattered or absorbed by the atmosphere --- direct solar radiation GD . Note that GD makes an angle with the surface of the earth because it's a direct ray from the sun to earth.

total radiation from the sun = Gsolar = GD cos θ + Gd

The molecules in the atmosphere emit radiation. This emission is not a blackbody emission, but in problem solving we treat it as blackbody emission at some lower temperature Tsky .

Gsky = σ Tsky4

For earth's surface, we can assume ε = α as long as the temperature is not changing much.

However, the surface will react differently to solar radiation and infrared radiation, so there's two separate set of ε numbers.
