Radiation Heat Transfer: Black Surfaces

The black surface case is the easiest because there is no reflection involved. If we have two black surfaces, Q1->2 = (heat radiated from surface 1 to surface 2) - (heat radiated from surface 2 to surface 1).

Q = A1 F1à2 σT14 - A2 F2à1 σT24

and A1 F12 = A2 F21 , so

Q = A1 F1à2 σ(T14 - T24)

 

Radiation Heat Transfer: Diffuse, Gray Surfaces

Here, we do take into account reflection, but we still make the simplification that the surfaces involved are opaque (not transparent), diffuse, and gray.

For a black body, the energy radiated is just: E = σ T4 , where E is in Watt per meter2 .To take into account reflection, we use a more complicated version of E, which we call J:

J = εE + ρG

J = εE + (1 - ε)G

Note that J has two components, one of which is E, and the other one is a reflected component of the incident radiation. The " ρ = 1 - ε " comes from assuming " α = ε ".

Radiation Shields and the Radiation Effect

Radiation heat transfer between two surfaces can be reduced greatly by inserting a thin, high reflectivity (low emissivity) sheet of material between the two surfaces --- a radiation shield. Multilayer radiation shields separated by evacuated space are commonly used in cryogenic and space applications.

Radiation Effect on Temperature Measurements

If there is a temperature difference between the fluid in a room and the walls of the room, a thermometer does not really measure the temperature of the fluid in the room.

Say the fluid temperature is higher than the wall temperature:

the fluid is then losing heat to the thermometer by convection, and the thermometer is losing heat to the wall by radiation.

If the temperature reading on the thermometer is not changing, then:

h(Tf - Tth) = εth σ(Tth4 - Tw4)

where the subscript "th" denote properties of the thermometer.

The difference between Tf and Tth is the most significant when h is small and ε is large.

If used outdoors, a thermometer must be protected from direct sunlight.

Because people lose a lot of heat to radiation, the temperature of the walls contributes significantly to the temperature felt by a person.

 

Radiation Exchange with Emitting and Absorbing Gases

So far, we have treated the gas as if it has no impact on radiation.

Gases that consist of monatomic molecules and symmetric diatomic molecules are essentially transparent to radiation. Gases with asymmetric molecules do participate in the radiation process. Two common examples are H2O and CO2 .

Special issues about how gases interact with radiation:

  • Gaseous radiation is volumetric phenomena.
  • Gas emit and absorb radiation at a number of narrow wavelength bands. Despite this, satisfactory results can be obtained by assuming the gas to be gray, using a single effective ε value for its emissivity.
  • How gas interacts with radiation depends on multiple factors: temperature, pressure, thickness of the gas layer, and composition of the gas mixture.

The radiation emissions of H2O and CO2 overlap in terms of wavelength. Therefore, the emissivity of a mixture of H2O and CO2 is not the isolated emissivities added together.

εg = εc + εw - ∆ε

εc is the ε for carbon dioxide, and εw is the ε for water.

∆ε is the emissivity correction factor, which accounts for the overlap of emission bands. ∆ε is plotted in figure 12-38 on page 644.
