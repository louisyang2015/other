Cooling of Electronic Equipment

Introduction

When current (I) flows through a resistance (R), the heat generated is I2 R.

Electronic circuits are powered by low voltages, usually under ±15 V, but the currents involved may be very high, sometimes a few hundred amperes.

Vacuum tubes had heat dissipation problems. When the transistor was first introduced, it was touted in the newspapers as a device that "produces no heat". But as the year go by and people pack millions of transistors onto a single chip, the heat dissipation problems reappears.

The failure rate of electronic devices increases almost exponentially with the operating temperature.

Cooling Load of Electronic Equipment

Duty cycle --- the fraction of time an electronic component is on. A 5W power transistor, for example, will dissipate an average of 2W of power if it is active only 40 percent of the time. Although most electronic devices operate for long periods of time, some applications never run long enough to reach steady operation. For these transient operation scenarios, a common approach is to have a double-wall construction for the enclosure of the electronic equipment. The space between the walls is filled with a wax with a suitable melting temperature. As the electronic device heats, the wax melts. If the device doesn't run for an extended period of time, the wax will have time to solidify itself.

Natural convection in space --- There is no natural convection in space because there is no gravity, and hence no buoyancy, in space.

The most effective ways of cooling include using forced convection of water, and dipping the hot object in a fluid and boiling the fluid.

 

Manufacturing of electronic equipment

In semiconductor physics, a junction is where the p material meets the n material. In heat transfer, a junction is a site of heat generation. It is the circuitry where electrons flow, and where the temperature is the highest.

The expansion coefficient of the plastic is about 20 times that of silicon. Therefore, bonding the silicon chip directly to the plastic case would result in too much thermal stress. Instead, the chip is bounded to a lead frame and the lead frame is bounded to the plastic frame.

The inside of the case is usually filled with a gas that is a poor conductor of heat. Heat going through the gas is modeled by having a junction-to-case resistance (Rjunction-case) .

Because we don't want moisture go get to the chip, the casing is highly sealed.

Fan Selection Issues

A fan at a fixed speed (or fixed rpm) will deliver a fixed volume of air regardless of the altitude and pressure. But the mass flow rate of air will be less at high altitude and low pressure due to the lower density of air.

Fan draw in not just cooler air but also all kinds of contaminants, so it's important to use a filter along with a fan. These contaminates can have poor heat conductivity factors, so if they get on top of components, they can cause the components to over heat.

It is usually best to place the fan at the inlet, rather than the exit of an electronic box. Placing the fan at the inlet makes it easier to filter the air going into the enclosure because if we placed the fan at the exit, then there could be multiple pathways for air to enter the enclosure. A fan at the inlet is more reliable because the fan itself is subjected to cool air.

The main problem with a fan at the inlet is that the heat generated by the fan and its motor is picked up by the air on its way into the box, which adds to the heat load of the system.

One thing a fan does is to create a volume flow of air into the electronic box. Another thing that the fan does is to create a pressure drop from the inlet to the exit. From the view point of the fan, a fan has limited power and it can deliver greater volume flow of air with a smaller pressure drop. From the view point of the electronic box, there is a certain level of resistance to "wind" and a greater volume flow of air requires a greater pressure drop. These two different trends are satisfied at one particular operating point, a pressure and flow rate combination that is in both the behavior of the fan and the behavior of the system:

Place the most critical electronic components near the entrance, where the air is the coolest.

Arrange the circuit boards and electronic components in the box such that the resistance of the box to airflow is minimized. Make sure that no hot air pockets are formed during operation.

Arrange the system such that natural convection helps forced convection rather than hurt it:

If the system has a lot of resistance to "wind", then have two fans in series. If the target is larger volume flow, use two fans in parallel.

 

Liquid Cooling

Circuit boards with up to about 5 W of power (or that have a power density of about 0.02 W/cm2 can be cooled effectively by natural convection.

Liquids normally have much higher thermal conductivities than gases. A boiling liquid can also drain heat very quickly. Liquid can be used to directly on electronic components --- immersing the chip in a liquid, but in these cases the liquid needs to be a dielectric liquid, so no water. Liquid can be used to indirectly cool electronic components, and this approach can be done with cheap liquid like water.

In a car engine, water circulates through the passages around the cylinders of the engine block, absorbing heat generated in the cylinders by combustion. The heated water is then routed by the water pump to the radiator, where it is cooled by air blown through the radiator coils by the cooling fan.

In an open loop system, the liquid is vaporized over time due to the heating from the electronic component. In a close loop system, we collect the vapor and condense it back into the liquid.

Fluorocarbon fluids, such as FC78 and FC86 have boiling points ranging from 30 oC to 174 oC. They are nonflammable, chemically inert, and highly compatible with materials used in electric equipment. They can achieve very high heat transfer rates.
