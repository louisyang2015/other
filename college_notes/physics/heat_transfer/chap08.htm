Internal Forced Convection

Internal forced convection is about fluid flowing in a pipe.

 

Mean Velocity (Vm)

When fluid flows in a pipe, the layer of fluid touching the inside of the pipe is actually not moving, and the fluid in the middle of the pipe is moving the fastest (Vmax) .

In problems they usually give you the rate of flow in terms of volume per second. In this case:
Vm * (cross sectional area of pipe) = Volume per second rate of flow

 

Mean Temperature (Tm) and Bulk Mean Temperature (Tb)

There's also a temperature profile in the flow.

The velocity profile and Vm stay the same in the direction of flow, but the temperature profile and Tm changes in the direction of flow. So Tm is the mean temperature only for a particular cross section of the pipe.

The mean temperature for the entire pipe is called the bulk mean fluid temperature (Tb). It is the average of the mean temperature at the inlet and outlet of the pipe.

Tb = (Tm,i + Tm,e) / 2

We evaluate the properties of the fluid at this Tb temperature.

The value of Re tells us what kind of flow it is:

Re < 2300 laminar flow
2300 ≤ Re ≤ 10,000 transitional flow
Re > 10,000 turbulent flow

 

The Entrance Region

This is how velocity changes in the entrance region.

Notice the velocity boundary layer will eventually meet in the center of the pipe. Prior to the boundary layer meeting, we say we are in the hydrodynamic entrance region. The length of this region is called the hydrodynamic entry length (Lh) .

Similarly we also get a thermal boundary layer that eventually meets in the middle. The length of the thermal entrance region is called thermal entry length (Lt).

Both the friction coefficient (f) and heat transfer coefficient (h) are highest at the inlet. The effect of the entrance region is always to enhance the average friction and heat transfer coefficients.

Formulas for Entry Lengths

Laminar flow:

Lh, laminar ≈ 0.05 Re D
Lt, laminar ≈ 0.05 Re Pr D

Lh, turbulent ≈ Lt, turbulent ≈ 10 D
where D is the diameter of the tube

Thermally Fully Developed Region --- This is the region after the thermal boundary layer meets in the middle. In this region, both the surface temperature TS and mean temperature Tm changes as a function of position, but the temperature profile (TS - T) / (TS - Tm) remains constant, and the heat transfer coefficient also remains constant.

Nusselt Numbers (Nu)

Long before "x" reach 10D, the Nu values settles out. Also, the Nu values are very alike for both "surface temperature (TS)" = constant conditions and "heat flux (qS)" = constant conditions.

Ignoring the Entrance Length --- In practice, the tubes are usually several times longer than either entrance region, and thus the flow through the tubes is often assumed to be fully developed for the entire length of the tube. This approach gives reasonable results for long tubes and underestimates the heat transfer (conservative results) for short tubes.

 

General Thermal Analysis

Heat Transfer to a Fluid Flowing through a Pipe:

Q = m CP (Te - Ti)

Q = heat transferred from the pipe to the fluid
m = flow rate in terms of mass per second
Te = mean temperature at the pipe exit
Ti = mean temperature at the pipe inlet

Constant Surface Heat Flux (qS = constant)

This situation can happen if the tube is subjected to radiation or electrical resistance heating.

We have:

qS = hx (TS - Tm)

The hx is the local heat transfer coefficient. It is approximately constant --- due to the Nu number being mostly constant. Since qS is also constant, then TS - Tm is constant.

m = fluid flow in terms of mass per unit time
CP = specific heat
Te = mean temperature at the exit
Ti = mean temperature at the inlet

Constant Surface Temperature (qS = constant)

This situation can happen if there is phase change, such as boiling or condensation at the outer surface of the tube.

Here's a graph of the temperature as a function of position:

The mean temperature approach TS exponentially. This is because the greater the temperature difference Tm(x) - TS , the greater the heat transfer, and so faster the Tm approach TS . When Tm is closer to TS , the heat transfer is not as great any more and so Tm will not change as quickly as before.

If we take a small slice of the fluid flowing through the tube:

m CP dTm = h (TS - Tm ) dAS

m = flow rate in terms of mass per unit volume
Cp = specific heat

The left term is the heat change as a slice of fluid move through the tube. The right term is the heat transferred from the wall of the pipe to the slice of fluid.

Exit temperature when surface temperature TS = constant:

Te = TS - (TS - Ti ) exp( (-h AS) / (m Cp) )

Te = mean temperature at the exit of the pipe
Ti = mean temperature at the inlet of the pipe
TS = temperature on the surface of the pipe
AS = surface area of the pipe
m = flow rate in terms of mass per second

We can let Te be any point beyond Ti if we just modify AS to be the surface area of the pipe from the Ti point to the Te point.

The "(-h AS) / (m Cp)" term is called NTU (number of transfer units). When NTU > 5, Tm(x) ≈ Ti and the heat transfer from the wall of the pipe to the fluid is minimal. Extending the pipe beyond NTU > 5 will not change heat transfer.

Flow through Tube Annulus

In this situation, one fluid flows through the central tube, while the other fluid flows outside of the central smaller tube and inside a larger tube.

Dh = 4 * AC / p

Dh = Do - Di

There are two Nu numbers, Nui for the inner tube's surface and Nuo for the outer tube's surface. For fully developed turbulent flow, these two Nu numbers are equal to one another. Compute the Nu by first using the formula for Nu in turbulent flow, and then multiplying the result with the following correction factor F :

F = 0.86 * (Di / Do)-0.16
