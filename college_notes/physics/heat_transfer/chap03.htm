The overall heat transfer coefficient (U) is defined as:

Q = UA ΔT

Thermal contact resistance

This is an extra resistance that you get at the boundary between two materials. The reason is that the materials are not smooth, so at their boundary there are air gaps that act as insulators.

The thermal contact resistance Rc is defined as:

Rc =
ΔTinterface
Q/A

Note that Rc is resistance per area. Larger interface area is less resistance, so R = Rc / A .

A surface that is less rough will produce a smaller Rc .

Two surfaces that are squished together with greater pressure will produce less Rc .

Most experimentally determined value of the thermal contact resistance Rc fall between 0.000005 and 0.0005 m2 oC / W. So Rc is significant if the materials involved are good thermal conductors, such as metals.

Rc can be minimized by applying a thermally conducting liquid called a thermal grease on the surfaces before they are pressed against eachother. We can also try to replace the air in the air gaps with a better conducting gas. Finally, we can also put a thin metallic foil between the two surfaces.

There is considerable uncertainty in the contact conductance data reported in the literature, and care should be exercised when using them.
