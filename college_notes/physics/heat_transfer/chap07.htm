Drag

Drag is the force that a flowing fluid exerts on an object. Drag is divided into two components --- one is the shearing force exerted by the fluid as it move over an object, the other is the pressure exerted by the fluid as it move against an object.

Drag is modeled by:

CD =
FD
0.5 ρ V2 A

CD = drag coefficient
FD = drag force
ρ = density
V = upstream velocity
A = "frontal area". For blunt objects, objects that tends to block the flow, the drag is due mostly to pressure and A is the area projected on a plane normal to the direction of flow. For plates or airfoils, the drag is due mostly to shearing drag, and A is defined as the surface area that is exposed to fluid flow.

Fluid Flow across Cylinders and Spheres

If the fluid flow's velocity is very, very slow, then the fluid lines will stay with the surface.

But most speeds, the fluid lines will separate from the surface of the body.

The lines separate from the surface at separation point, and the middle line will recombine at the reattachment point. The separation region has low pressure. The wake region has lower fluid velocity and extends far beyond the separation region.

The fluid particles on the midplane strike the cylinder at the stagnation point --- pressure is maximum at the stagnation point

The characteristic length "L" for a sphere is the external diameter "D", so Re is:

Rex =
V D
μ/ρ

The flow is laminar until separation if Re ≤ 2 * 105 . But if Re ≥ 2 * 105 , the flow will include both laminar and turbulent regions before separation.

In the turbulent region of the boundary layer of the second case, drag is lower than the corresponding separation region of the first case. That is how rough surfaces on golf balls can reduce drag as compared with smooth balls.
