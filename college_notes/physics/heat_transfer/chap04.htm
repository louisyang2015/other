Transient Heat Conduction

This chapter deals with problems where the temperature is changing as a function of time. Of course, when temperature changes with time, the heat flow is also changing with time.

 

Lumped System Analysis

Lumped system analysis means that we treat the object as having uniform temperature distribution --- that the temperature is the same everywhere in an object.

A picture of the lumped system situation:

The object starts out at a uniform temperature Ti , with the environment at temperature T∞ . The object eventually takes on the temperature of the environment. During the change from Ti to T∞ , the temperature is uniform throughout the object . Heat is convected to the body, and then conducted within the body.

θ is called the excess temperature. As T(t) changes from Ti to T∞ , θ changes from 1 to 0.

When to use lumped system analysis?

To have approximately uniform temperature, we need a high k so that heat flows quickly within an object, and a low so that the amount of perturbation to the object's energy level is minimized.

The rest of this chapter does not use the lumped system assumption. Objects are assumed to have a uniform temperature at the beginning, when t = 0, but the temperatures at different parts of the object will differ for t > 0.

Transient Heat Conduction in Semi-Infinite Solids

In the semi-infinite solids model, we are trying to find the temperature at a point just under the surface of a solid. We have an x-axis that is going into the solid, and if the point is close enough to the surface of the solid, we can treat the surface as an infinitely large surface. This is so that one surface alone is responsible for the temperature change.

Just like the previous sections, the environment is at temperature T∞ , the solid has initial temperature Ti . The solid has convection coefficient h and conduction coefficient k.

If h is very large or infinite, then the surface temperature of the solid object will equal the environmental temperature T∞ in a very short amount of time.

What's new is that the object also has thermal diffusivity α . Thermal diffusivity was defined in chapter 2.

Overlapping Geometries

If two geometries overlap to form a new geometry, the θ for the new geometry is the product of the two θ values for the old geometry.

For example, a short cylinder is the overlap of an infinitely long cylinder and a infinitely large wall.

This product rule works for geometries that are formed by overlapping three basic geometries as well.

A table from the book shows more example of this product rule. Note that the x coordinate is measured from the surface in a semi-infinite solid, and from the midplane in a plane wall.
