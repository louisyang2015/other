Minor Remarks

Chapter 1, Basics of Heat Transfer, is about stuff that is already mostly covered in the Thermodynamics section of Physics.

Table 1 and Table 15 in the appendix contains important information about air.

There is a type of problem where the air enters through one end of a tunnel and comes out in the other end. The temperature in the air is different in the two ends. The problem will as you how much heat is transferred in the problem. First, find out the volume of air going through the tunnel. Air has different specific heat at different temperatures. Use the specific heat corresponding to the average temperature in the tunnel.

The ideal gas law: " PV = nRT " can do several different things based on the choice of units for R. The book lists seven different "R"s in the back. For example, by choosing the right R, n can be mass, and n/V can be density.

Table 15 only lists the specific heat with the pressure held constant (Cp). So you must know:

CV = CP - R

Heat transfer through a vacuum uses radiation only.

Heat transfer through an opaque solid uses conduction only.

Heat transfer through gas usually use radiation. Some gases are known to absorb radiation in certain wavelengths, like how the ozone absorbs ultraviolet light. But in general gases don't absorb radiation. In addition to radiation, Heat transfer through gas use either conduction (still fluid) or convection (flowing fluid).
