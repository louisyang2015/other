Fundamentals of Convection

Fluid over a Surface

When a fluid flows across a surface, the bottom most layer of the fluid will have a velocity of zero. This is called the no-slip condition.

Additionally, there's friction between layers of fluid --- so the bottom most layer slows the layer on top of it, which in turn slows the layer on top of it.

This friction in between layers of fluid is internal resistance to the flow of the fluid. It is called viscosity. Some fluids, such as oil, have high viscosity and are called viscous flows. The opposite is frictionless or inviscid flows.

Velocity Boundary Layer

Given a fluid flowing across a surface, the lowest layer of the fluid that is touching the surface will have velocity zero, and the layers above the lowest layer will have increasing velocity. At some layer sufficiently far above the surface the fluid velocity will reach some maximum value u∞ . The velocity boundary layer is the place before the fluid velocity reach this u∞ . The velocity boundary thickness δ is defined as the distance y from the surface at which u = 0.99u∞ .

In the velocity boundary layer, each layer of fluid is moving at a different speed, and so there is friction between the different layers. Above the velocity boundary layer, there is not much friction between layers of fluid --- so we call that the inviscid flow region.

Thermal Boundary Layer

Similar to the velocity boundary layer there is a thermal boundary layer. This is for the case where the fluid temperature is different from the surface temperature. At sufficient distance from the surface, the temperature will be the fluid temperature T∞ . At the surface we have the temperature TS . The thermal boundary layer is a zone where the temperature varies from TS to TS + 0.99 * (T∞ - TS).

The velocity boundary layer and the thermal boundary layer ARE NOT identical. The friction between layers of fluid does cause some heating --- but this heating is very small is treated as zero. So there is no easy link between the thermal boundary and the velocity boundary. Most of the heating or cooling is due to the surface temperature being different from the fluid temperature, not due to friction between layers of fluid. There is an exception, if Pr ≈ 1, then the two boundary layers coincide each other.
