Kinetic Theory of Gases

The kinetic theory of gas says that the kinetic energy of molecular motion and the internal energy of a gas system are equivalent.

m = mass of a single molecule in this chapter. Previously, m has been used to represent the mass of an entire sample.

Theorem of Equipartition of Energy

Each degree of freedom contributes (1/2)(kB T) to the energy of a system

Monatomic molecule

For a monatomic molecule, the internal energy is just translational energy. There are three degrees of freedom --- the molecule can translate in the x, y, or z direction, and so there are three degrees of freedom.

Diatomic molecule

For a diatomic molecule, the two atoms can rotate about the x, y, or z axis. Rotation about the y axis produce little kinetic energy --- so that can be ignored. The rotational aspect of the diatomic molecule contributes two degrees of freedom.

Also, the two molecules are modeled as having a "spring" in between them. The vibration aspect of the diatomic molecule is considered as two additional degrees of freedom.

Energy Quantization

Due to energy quantization, if an energy is too small, it can not exist. Under low temperatures, the rotational and vibrational energies are so small that they do not exist for diatomic molecules. Under medium (room) temperatures, diatomic gas molecules both translate and rotate, but still there is no vibration. It is only under high temperature that the diatomic molecule vibrates.

Solids

A molecule in a solid lattice can translate and vibrate in the x, y, and z directions. So a solid's molecule has six degrees of freedom. Once again, the molecule only gets all six degrees of freedom if the temperature is sufficiently high.

 

Molar Specific Heat

When we apply heat to gas, a portion of the heat goes to increasing the temperature of the gas, while a portion of it expands the gas and does work. So there is potentially many different specific heats for a gas, depending on how much of the applied heat is used to raise temperature.

  • If we hold the volume of the gas constant, all of the heat goes to rising the temperature of the gas
  • If we hold the pressure of the gas constant, the temperature of the gas goes up and the gas is doing work by expanding.
The two important specific heats for gas:

Q = n CV ΔT (constant volume)
Q = n CP ΔT (constant pressure)

n --- the number of moles in the sample

CV --- molar specific heat at constant volume
CP --- molar specific heat at constant temperature

In the case of solids CV = CP because the work done by the expansion of the solid is minimum.

Under the constant volume scenario, there is no work done. Q = ∆Eint

Adiabatic Process

In an adiabatic process, the system is insulated so that no energy leave the system by heat (Q = 0). The system does work on the outside world, but does not heat or cool the outside world.

During an adiabatic process:

PVY = constant

Y = CP / CV
