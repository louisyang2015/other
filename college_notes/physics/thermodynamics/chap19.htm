Temperature

Heat is the transfer of energy from one object to another object as a result of a difference in temperature between the two.

Thermal contact does not necessarily require physical contact. Two objects are in thermal contact if energy can be exchanged between them, even if the objects are not touching.

Thermal equilibrium is a situation where two objects are in thermal contact with each other but they cease to exchange energy by the process of heat. Two objects in thermal equilibrium with each other are at the same temperature.

The zeroth law of thermodynamics --- If objects A and B are separately in thermal equilibrium with a third object C, then objects A and B are in thermal equilibrium with each other.

ice point of water --- This is the degree zero on the Celsius scale. This is a mixture of water and ice in thermal equilibrium at atmospheric pressure.

steam point of water --- This is the 100th degree on the Celsius scale. This is a mixture of water and steam in thermal equilibrium at atmospheric pressure.

 

Constant-Volume Gas Thermometer

A very accurate way to measure temperature is to confine gas to a constant volume, and then measure the pressure exerted by this gas. Higher temperature corresponds to higher pressure.

It turns out that the graphs of multiple gases will converge to a point of absolute zero where the gas exerts no pressure.

triple point of water --- In this environment, liquid water, gaseous water, and ice all coexist in equilibrium. This is 0.01o C, pressure of 4.58 mm of mercury. This point is set to be 273.16 Kelvin

For small changes in temperature, the thermal expansion is approximately linearly related to temperature:

bimetallic strip --- This is two thin strips of dissimilar metals bonded together. As the temperature increases, the two metals expand by different amounts, and the strip as a whole bends.

 

The unusual behavior of water

From 0o C to 4o C water contracts and its density increases. In other words, the density of water reach a maximum value of 1000 kg/m3 at 4o C.

This property is why ponds begin to freeze at the surface rather than at the bottom. As water hit the zero to four degree range, the warmer water sinks and the colder water floats to the top. So the colder water go to a colder place while the warmer water goes to the warmer place.

 

Macroscopic Description of an Ideal Gas

The environment for ideal gas:

The ideal gas law:

PV = nRT

P is pressure of the gas
V is volume occupied by the gas
n is the number of moles of gas molecules there are. Each mole of gas is NA =  6.022 * 1023 molecules of gas.
R is the universal gas constant = 8.315 J / (mol * K) = 0.08214 (L * atm) / (mol * K)
T is the temperature of the gas

Using the ideal gas law, 1 mol of any gas at atmospheric pressure and at 0o C (273 K) is 22.4 L

Another version of the ideal gas law:

PV = NkBT

N is the number of molecules of gas
kB is the Boltzmann's constant = 1.38 * 10-23 J / K

The ideal gas law is true for real gases at low pressure (or low density).
