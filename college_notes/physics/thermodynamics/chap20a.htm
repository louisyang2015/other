Heat

Internal energy is the energy related to an substance's molecular structure. It includes kinetic energy of translation, rotation, and vibration of molecules, potential energy within molecules, and potential energy between molecules. Internal energy does not include any bulk kinetic energy of the substance as that substance moves through space.

You do not necessary need to have heat to transfer internal energy. For example, if you compress gas, you are increasing the internal energy of the gas without adding any heat to it. The temperature of the compressed gas is higher, but it's not due to a transfer of heat because the higher temperature does not come from a temperature difference between the gas and the surrounding.

 

Units of heat

calorie (cal) --- the amount of energy transfer necessary to raise the temperature of 1 g of water from 14.5oC to 15.5oC. Calorie, written with capital "C" and used in describing the energy content of foods, is actually a kilocalorie.

British thermal unit (btu) --- the amount of energy transfer required to raise the temperature of 1 lb of water from 63oF to 64oF

Joule established the equivalence between heat and mechanical energy:

1 cal = 4.186 J

 

Specific Heat

Example: The specific heat of liquid water at 15oC is 4186 J / (kg oC). That means it takes 4186 J of energy to increase one kilogram of water by one degree.

The general equation for applying heat to a substance:

Q = c * m * ΔT

Q --- the heat applied to a substance
c --- the specific heat number for the substance
m --- the mass of the substance
∆T --- the change in temperature of the substance

(c * m)  is called the heat capacity of a particular substance

Strictly speaking, specific heat varies with temperature. But the this detail will be ignored.

Water has by far the highest specific heat of common materials.

Calorimetry --- this is the measurement of specific heat. One way is to mix a substance X at a higher temperature (TX) with water at a lower temperature (Twater) . The substance and the water then mix to some final temperature (Tfinal) between TX and Twater.

Heat gained by water = heat loss by the substance X

cwater * mwater * (Tfinal - Twater) = cX * mX * (TX - Tfinal)

 

Latent Heat

Latent heat of fusion --- the amount of energy needed to melt a substance's solid form into liquid form

Latent heat of vaporization --- the amount of energy needed to vaporize a substance's liquid form into gas form

Q = m * L

Q --- the heat it takes to melt or vaporize something
m --- mass of the substance being melted or vaporized
L --- latent heat number for the object

The latent heat of vaporization is usually greater than the latent heat of fusion. The reason is that the separation between molecules in the gas stage is much greater than the separation in either the liquid or solid phase.

During the melting and vaporization phase, the temperature of the substance does not change as we apply heat to it. So if we apply heat (Q) to a piece of cold ice, the temperature of the ice (T) would pause during the melting phase and the vaporization phase.
