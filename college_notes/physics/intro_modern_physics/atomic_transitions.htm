Atomic Transitions

An atom emits electromagnetic radiation if an electron in an excited state makes a transition to a lower energy state. The set of wave lengths observed is called the emission spectrum.

In reverse, atoms with electrons in the ground state configuration can also absorb electromagnetic radiation at specific wavelengths, giving rise to an absorption spectrum.

While electrons can move from one state to another, not all changes of states are equally likely. The vast majority of state changes obey the selection rules for allowed transitions:

∆ = ±1

∆ = 0 or ±1

 

When an electron changes state, its angular momentum changes. Angular momentum must be conserved, so we have angular momentum going from the electron to the photon. The photon has an angular momentum equivalent to that of a particle having a spin of 1.

X-Ray Spectra

X rays are emitted if a metal target is bombarded by high energy electrons, or any charged particle.

Assume that the incoming electron dislodged an electron from the inner most shell, the K shell where n = 1. If the vacancy is filled by an electron dropping from the next higher shell, which is the L shell, where n = 2, the photon emitted in the process has an energy corresponding to the Kα peak shown in the above graph. If this graph is pictured as a spectrum, Kα would be a band on that spectrum.

Likewise, the Kβ peak is produced when an electron in the K shell is dislodged, and an electron from the M shell (where n = 3) fills the vacancy.

If we dislodged an electron in the L shell (n = 2):

  • an electron falling from the M shell (n = 3) to fill the vacancy will produce a Lα peak
  • an electron falling from the N shell (n = 4) to fill the vacancy will produce a Lβ peak

Specific types of electron transitions

Stimulated Absorption

  1. Electron is in ground state
  2. Photon comes by and is absorbed by the electorn
  3. Electron goes into an excited state

Spontaneous Emission

  1. Normally electron stay excited for just 10-8 second.
  2. The excited electron will fall back to its ground state and emit a photon in the process.

Stimulated Emission

  1. The atom remain in an excited state for much longer than 10-8 second.
  2. The excited electron stay excited until it gets hit by a photon.
  3. The photon does not excite the electron further, but "convinces" the electron to return to its ground state.
  4. The electron falls to the ground state and release a photon.
  5. There are now two photons flying around. The original photon that hit the electron and the recently emitted photon.

Normally most atoms are in there ground state.

Population inversion --- Under this condition, there are more atoms in the excited state than in the ground state. When a light beam comes, it would trigger a net emission of photons.

Lasers work by first having a material in the state of population inversion. And then a small beam of incoming light will trigger a much larger emission of light. Some of the emitted light is reflected back at the material as a feed back to keep the reaction going.
