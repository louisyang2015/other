Electrical Conduction in Metals, Insulators, and Semiconductors

Metals:

If an electric field is applied to the metal, electrons having energies near the Fermi energy requires only small amount of additional energy from the electric field to reach nearby empty energy states.

Insulators:

Unlike metals, insulators have a large band gap energies (≈ 10eV) between the conduction band and the valence band.

Semiconductors

Semiconductors have a band gap energy (Eg) of ≈ 1 eV.

At room temperature, some electrons from the valence band will make it into the conduction band. At higher temperatures, conductivity will improve further.

An electron that moved from the valence band to the conduction band will leave behind a hole. In a pure crystal or intrinsic semiconductor, the number of holes equal the number of electrons.

Doped Semiconductor

When an atom containing five outer-shell electrons, such as arsenic, is added to a semiconductor, four of the valence electrons participate in the covalent bonds and one is left over. Arsenic is called a donor atom and the donated electron lie just slightly below the conduction band in terms of energy. Doping with arsenic produces an n-type semiconductor.

Aluminum has three valence electrons. Doping with aluminum produce a hole to accept electrons, so the aluminum atom is called an acceptor atom.

The energy band analogy for aluminum doping is a bit awkward: a hole is floating right above the valence band. Give it some energy and the hole will drop into the valence band and conduct.

Doping with aluminum produces a p-type semiconductor.

When conduction is dominated by acceptor or donor impurities, the material is called an extrinsic semiconductor.
