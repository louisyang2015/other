Blackbody Radiation and Planck's Hypothesis

An object at any temperature emits radiation --- called thermal radiation.

A blackbody is an ideal object that absorbs all radiation incident on it. So with a blackbody, the radiation coming from it is due entirely to its temperature. A good approximation to a blackbody is the inside of a hollow object.

There are two trends. One is that higher temperature results in more radiation.

The second trend is that the peak intensity is shifting to a shorter wavelength as the temperature increases. Low temperature objects emit mostly infrared light, which can't be seen. Higher temperature objects will glow blue or white.

The location of the peak intensity is given by the formula:

λmax * T = 0.2898 * 10-2 (meter * Kelvin)

λmax --- the wave length at which the blackbody radiation at temperature T peaks.

Failure of the Classical Model
