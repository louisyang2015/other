The Photoelectric Effect

Experiments showed that light incident on certain metallic surfaces caused electrons to be emitted from the surfaces. Classical physics failed to give the proper results --- and Einstein came up with a solution in 1905:

  • Light, or any other electromagnetic wave, of frequency f, can be considered a stream of photons. Each photons has energy E = hf.

A photon is so localized that it gave all of its energy hf to a single electron in the metal.

The maximum kinetic energy for these liberated electrons is:

	kmax = hf - Φ

where Φ is called the work function of the metal. The work function represents the minimum energy with which an electron is bound in the metal.

If the hf of the incoming photon is not ≥ Φ, the electrons are never ejected from the surface, regardless of light intensity. It is the type of light that determines whether electrons get ejected.

The intensity of the light determines how many photons are sent at the metal. So if a particular type of light that is capable of ejecting electrons from the metal, more intense light of that type will eject more electrons from the metal.

So while the type of light determines how a single photon interacts with a single electron, the intensity of light determines how many photons interact with an equal number of electrons.
