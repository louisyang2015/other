A Particle in a Well

As in the "particle in a box" scenario, the particle has zero potential energy in the one dimensional interval 0 < x < L. Also like before, there are barriers at the ends of the interval. Unlike before, these barriers have a finite potential energy that is higher than the energy of the particle.

A classical analogy for the particle in a well situation is to have a ball that is moving with kinetic energy E.

If the energy E of the particle is less than U, that ball can't escape the well by rolling up the slope. But in quantum mechanics, the particle does have a small chance of existing outside of the well.

Tunneling Through a Barrier

Consider a particle of energy E incident on a rectangular barrier of height U and width L, where E < U

Classically, E < U means the particle is stuck in region I, and can't go into region II, III.

But the results from the "particle in a well" scenario says that the wave function Ψ will be sinusoidal in region I, decaying exponentially in region II, and sinusoidal again in region III.

The probability of a particle tunneling through a barrier can be described with a transmission coefficient, T, and a reflection coefficient, R. T is the probability that a particle penetrates the barrier, and R is the probability that the particle is reflected by the barrier. T + R = 1
