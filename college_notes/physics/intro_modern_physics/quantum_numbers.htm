The Exclusion Principle and the Periodic Table

The exclusion principle --- No two electron can ever be in the same quantum state. No two electron in the same atom can have the same set of quantum numbers: n, , , ms

Hund's rule --- When an atom has orbitals of equal energy, the order in which they are filled by the electron is such that a maximum number of electrons have unpaired spins. The idea is to avoid having electrons in the same orbital (box) until as late as possible, so that Coulombic potential energy from two nearby electrons is minimized.

The silver (Ag) atom has one spin-wise unpaired electron in the 5s shell. But this unpaired electron is donated to an electron cloud that is shared by the entire metal. As a result, individual silver atoms do not really have spin-wise unpaired electrons and silver is not magnetic.

The iron (Fe) atom has an outer most shell of 3d6 and therefore have 4 spin-wise unpaired electrons. At least two of these are retained by the iron atom and not donated to the electron cloud. As a result, iron is magnetic.

That yellow spot that I marked is Cr (chromium). Counting based on solely the periodic table would give an outer shells of 4s2 3d4 . Experimentally, the outer shells are 4s1 3d5 .

It turns out that the 4s level is only slightly lower in energy than the 3d. By having one electron in 4s and 5 in 3d, we avoid having paired electrons. It's hund's rule at work --- that avoiding paired electrons would be worth the trouble of sending a 4s electron into the 3d level.
