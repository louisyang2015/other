The Uncertainty Principle

If a measurement of position is made with precision ∆x and a simultaneous measurement of momentum component px is made with precision ∆p, then:

Δx Δp ≥
h
4π

The other version of the uncertainty principle is about measuring the energy of a system E over a finite interval of time ∆t.

ΔE Δt ≥
h
4π

∆t is the time spent in the measurement, not the error with the clock. So if we spent a lot of time measuring the energy of a system, we get to reduce ∆E.

These errors do not arrise from imperfection in the measuring instrument. Rather, it arise from the quantum structure of matter.

Example:

Say that we are trying to determine an electron's position by looking at it. In order to be seen, a photon must hit the electron and bounce back to the viewer. This photon tells you where the electron is, but in doing so, it changed the electron's momentum. When the photon returns to the eye, it can determine the position of the electron to within one wave length. So we have ∆p = h / λ, ∆x = λ, and the product is h.

Say that we are trying to measure a 1000 Hz electrical wave. Our frequency measuring device has a fixed phase sensitivity of ± 1 cycle. In one second we measure 1000 ± 1 cycles. In 2 seconds we measure 2000 ± 1 cycles which leads us to conclude that the frequency is 1000 ± 0.5 cycles. The uncertainty in the frequency is inversely proportional to the time spent on the measurement. We have ∆f * ∆t = 1, which leads to ∆E * ∆t = h
