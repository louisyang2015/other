41.3 The Double-Slit Electron Experiment

The experiment is to shoot a beam of electrons at a wall with two slits. An electron collector far from the wall detects how many electrons arrive and at what location.

The collector will detect an interference pattern. But before we study this interference pattern, let's look at the basic case of a single slit.

If the slit is narrow enough such that there is no single slit diffraction, then the electron detector will see a bell shape like pattern where most of the electron land directly in front of the slit. Let's call this pattern the blue curve. If electrons are purely particles, and do not interfere with one another, then in the case of two slits, the electron detector should detect a pattern that is the sum of two blue curves.

Instead of the accumulation of two blue curves, the experiment actually detects a red curve (which looks like reddish brown in the scanned picture).

This red curve can be predicted by standard wave theory.

For example, the minimums closest to center appear at places where the difference in distance is half a wave length: (B - A) = λ / 2
