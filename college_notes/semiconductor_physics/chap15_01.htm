Field Effect Introduction --- The J-FET and MESFET

General Introduction

The field effect transistor was first conceived in the 1920s. In late 1964, commercial MOSFETs are available from Fairchild Semiconductor and RCA. By the late 60s, DRAM is introduced as a one transistor dynamic memory cell. In the 80s, the CMOS became the integrated circuit technology of choice.

GaAs has electron transport properties that are superior to silicon, but GaAs lacks a quality homomorphic (Ga or As containing) insulator comparable to SiO2 . However, new technology is allowing the growth of different lattice-matched semiconductors on top of each other. The alloy semiconductor AlGaAs can assume the role of "insulator" because it has a higher band gap than GaAs. This results in the MODFET, touted to be the fastest three terminal semiconductor device.

 

J-FET (Junction Field Effect Transistor)

The J-FET works by having a reverse bias between the p-type gate and the n-type channel. The amount of reverse bias will control the depletion region width between the p-type gate and the n-type channel, and this width controls the amount of current flowing from source to drain.

The carriers move from source to drain, so for an n-channel device, the current actually goes from drain to source.

Here is a more realistic picture of the J-FET.

Qualitative Theory of Operation

As the voltage grows across the n-channel, so does the depletion region.

As VDS increases, the upper and lower depletion regions will meet up at the drain end of the n-channel.

At first, the I-V relationship is basically linear --- more voltage will yield more current.

As the depletion regions widen, the loss of conductive medium will result in less additional current per additional voltage.

When the two depletion regions meet, we say that the n-channel is pinched off. The current saturates and additional voltage does not result in additional current.

Why does pinch-off not totally eliminate all current flow? --- Although the depletion region cuts through the channel, the existence of the depletion region does not mean there is no current. Depletion region  is not truly devoid of carriers, they just have few carriers compared to the background doping. Semiconductor physics is full of cases where large current flows through the depletion region. Also, if the current through the n-channel is zero, then the voltage everywhere in the n-channel is also zero, and the pinch-off condition would cease to exist. In other words, the flow of current is necessary to maintain the pinch off condition.

Why does the current saturate? --- The original channel length is L. The pinch-off reduces the channel length by ∆L. As long as L and L - ∆L are approximately the same, we have the same pinch-off voltage,  VDSat (5 volt), across nearly the same kind of channel. Voltages above the pinch-off voltage, VD - VDSat , exists across the depleted section of the channel and does not contribute to increasing current. For the short channel devices, L - ∆L is not the same as L; so that VDSat falls over a significantly shorter channel as VD increases, and we get a drain current that does not really saturate.

So far we set VG to zero so that the reverse bias above and below the channel is due purely to the drain voltage VD . If we set VG to less than zero, VG will help produce the depletion region above and below the channel. This decrease the drain current and lowers the VDSat .

At sufficiently negative VG = VP , even VD = 0 will result in the entire n-channel being depleted.
