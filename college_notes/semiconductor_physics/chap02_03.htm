Semiconductor Models

Development throughout this chapter assumes equilibrium conditions.

Equilibrium Conditions --- There are no external voltages, magnetic fields, stresses, or other perturbing forces acting on the semiconductor.

 

The Bonding Model

A silicon(Si) atom has 14 electrons. Ten of these electrons are in inner shells and they remain unperturbed during chemical reactions. Four electrons are in the valence shell. A picture of a bunch of Si atoms bonded together:

Each line represent one valence electron that is shared between two atoms.

The picture is 2D, but the actual structure is the diamond lattice mentioned in chapter one.

Such "perfect" lattice only happens at 0 K. Even small thermal agitation will break some of the bonds.

For an electron to become a carrier, the bond between two silicon atoms needs to be broken. Once the bond is broken, the electron is freed to move around. Freeing the electron also creates a hole in the Si lattice. This hole is also viewed as a current carrier.

Acceptors are elements from the periodic table's column III that has three valence electrons. If we replace a silicon atom with an atom from column III, then a hole is created because there is a shortage of one electron to complete the octet rule. Boron is the most popular acceptor. Acceptors make the semiconductor p-typed material, meaning that it has more holes than electrons. The majority carrier in p-typed material is the hole, while the minority carrier is the electron.

Donors are elements from the periodic table's column V that has five valence electrons. If we replace a silicon atom with an atom from column V, then an electron is created because the fifth valence electron is not needed to satisfy the octet rule. Phosphorus and arsenic are the most popular donors. Donors make the semiconductor n-typed material, meaning that it has more electrons than holes.

Donors do not create holes, they only bring electrons. When the phosphorus atom's fifth electron becomes a free electron, the phosphorous atom does get a positive charge. But since the atom is part of the crystal lattice and can not move, it is not a carrier.

By the same reasoning, when another electron fills in the missing bond at the site of the boron atom, the boron atom gets a negative charge but does not become a carrier.

 

The Energy Band Model

When a bunch of Si atoms come together, the allowed electron states for the valence shell split into two groups. The group that is lower in energy is called the valence band. The group that is higher in energy is called the conduction band. Electrons can exist in the valence band or the conduction band, but can not exist in between the two bands. The energy levels at the edge of the two bands are labeled EV and EC .

The energy between the two bands is the band gap energy EG.

EC - EV = EG

At room temperature:

EG = 1.42 eV for GaAs
EG = 1.12 eV for Si
EG = 0.66 eV for Ge

At 0 K, all electrons of the Si lattice is in the valence band. It would take 1.12 eV to move an atom from the valence band to the conductance band. An electron in the valence band is an electron that is part of the bonds between Si atoms --- it can not move. Once an electron gets into the conduction band, then it can move. An electron moving to the conduction band also leave behind a hole in the valence band, which is also a current carrier.

Donors and Acceptors in the Energy Band Model

Say an Si atom in the lattice is replaced by a donor atom. The fifth electron of the donor atom will initially want to stay with the donor because of attraction by the donor nucleus. Although the fifth electron does not take part in the bonding, it would still take a small amount of energy |EB| to free the fifth electron.

In the energy band model, the donor atom brings in an electron at an energy level |EB| less than EC . A slight amount of energy is needed to send this electron into the conduction band.

The effect of the acceptor is to introduce an empty state at an energy level EA that is slightly above EV . Then, even a small amount of energy can excite an electron from EV to the EA state, creating a hole in the valence band that act as a carrier.

 

Effective Mass

Let q = the elemental charge.

One way to describe the force felt by a charge is:

F = (charge) * (Electric Field felt by the charge)

Another way is to describe the force felt by a charge is to use Newton's Law:

F = (mass)
dv
dt

This (mass) quantity is different in different situations. In a vacuum, (mass) would be the mass of the charge. In a semiconductor, (mass) would be something else. Whatever (mass) that works in a particular medium is called the effective mass for that medium.

m0 = the electron rest mass = 9.11 * 10-31 kg

Effective Masses at 300 K

Material (mn*)/m0 (mp*)/m0
Si 1.18 0.81
Ge 0.55 0.36
GaAs 0.066 0.52

 

Carrier Numbers in Intrinsic Material

Let:

n = number of electrons / cm3

p = number of holes / cm3

Intrinsic material has not been doped, so n = p.

Let:

ni = number of electrons / cm3 in intrinsic materials

n = p = ni

At room temperature:

ni = 2.25 * 106 / cm3 in GaAs

ni = 1.45 * 1010 / cm3 in Si

The opposite of intrinsic semiconductor is the extrinsic semiconductor, also called a doped semiconductor.
