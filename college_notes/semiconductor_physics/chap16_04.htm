Capacitance under Depletion

Capacitance under Inversion

Under accumulation and depletion, the changes a MOS experience has to do with the creation or movement majority carriers. When majority carriers are involved the charge state can change very quickly according to the ac signal.

Under inversion, the changes a MOS experience has to do with the creation of minority carriers, which is sluggish. Capacitance under inversion behaves differently depending on the frequency of the ac signal that is applied to the MOS.

If the frequency is sufficiently low so that minority carriers can be generated or annihilated in response to the applied ac signal, then the extra charge in inversion will appear that the oxide - semiconductor boundary:

This case is just like accumulation and C(inv) ≈ CO .

If the frequency is sufficiently high that the minority can not be generated quickly enough, then the inversion is the same as the depletion. The charges will be created at a distance W from the oxide - semiconductor boundary. Unlike depletion, the W in inversion stopped increasing and capacitance is a constant.

The reason is that with higher doping, the semiconductor is more and more like a metal. At high enough doping, the MOS capacitor is just like a regular capacitor, which has a constant capacitance independent of the voltage across it.

Practical Observations

In modern day MOS-Cs, with their long carrier lifetimes and low carrier generation rates, even probing frequencies as low as 10 Hz will yield high frequency characteristics.

To get the VG versus C graph, we need to vary the dc bias (VG). In the following graph, we are sweeping from positive dc bias to negative dc bias, and R is the rate at which we vary the dc:

note that even if we decrease the dc bias slowly, ramp rate = 0.035 V per second, we still do not get the proper capacitance graph for the inversion zone. This is due to the extremely slow rate of minority carrier generation. Previously, we said that in the inversion bias, the ac signal will add charges at a distance of W from the oxide - semiconductor boundary. In this case, even the charges added by the dc signal is showing up at a distance of W from the oxide - semiconductor boundary instead of right at the oxide -semiconductor boundary.

This is a non-equilibrium condition called deep depletion. Minority carrier generation on the oxide - semiconductor border is too difficult or time consuming and the depletion region grows beyond its theoretical equilibrium value of WT .

To get the proper capacitance values, we need to periodically stop changing the dc signal and allow the device to settle into equilibrium. We can also sweep the device from inversion toward accumulation.
