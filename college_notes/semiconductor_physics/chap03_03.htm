Carrier Recombination-Generation

Recombination --- a process whereby electrons and holes (carriers) are annihilated or destroyed

Generation --- a process whereby electrons and holes are created

These terms don't refer to a particular process because the destruction and creation of carriers can happen in a number of different ways.

R-G Centers --- these are lattice defects that introduce electron states at an energy level ET near the middle of the band gap. They can act as sites for carrier recombination and carrier generation.

An electron can go to a R-G center, and a hole can go to a R-G center, then energy is released as heat. Or the R-G center can act as an intermediate stop for an electron falling to the valence band.

Auger Recombination --- two electrons (or carriers) collide. One lose energy and falls into a hole and they combine, the other electron gains energy and release the energy in small steps. This form of recombination can happen if the semiconductor is highly doped.

Methods of generation include:

If thermal energy is used to do band to band generation, it's called direct thermal generation. If light is used, it is called photogeneration.

The R-G center can act as intermediate stop for an electron rising to the conduction band.

Impact ionization --- When a highly energetic carrier collides with the crystal lattice, an electron-hole pair can be produced. This form of generation can happen if there is a very strong electric field inside the semiconductor.

 

Direct Semiconductors versus Indirect Semiconductors

During recombination/generation, both momentum and energy needs to be conserved.

Momentum is also quantized and allowed only in specific states.

A E-k plot, is an energy versus momentum plot, with E being the y-axis.

In E-k plots, the valence band maximum energy is always at k = 0

If the conduction band minimum energy is at k = 0, then the semiconductor is a direct semiconductor. If the minimum energy is somewhere else, then it's an indirect semiconductor.

GaAs is a direct semiconductor.
Si and Ge are both indirect semiconductors.

Holes tend to be at the maximum of the valence band energy while electrons tend to be at the minimum of the conduction band energy.

In the case of the direct semiconductor, the electron can combine with a hole with minimum change in momentum. But in the case of the indirect semiconductor, the electron changes both momentum and energy.

Band-to-band recombination emits a photon in the process, and photon has very little momentum (photon has no mass). Band-to-band recombination can not happen frequently in indirect semiconductors because of the momentum requirement.

The R-G center processes emits phonons - which has mass. So R-G center processes can satisfy the momentum requirement in indirect semiconductors.

For indirect semiconductors, band-to-band recombination is negligible compared to R-G center recombination. Direct semiconductors see a lot more band-to-band recombination, but even there R-G center recombination is usually dominant.

Photo Generation

If we shine light on a material, if the photon energy (h*frequency) is greater than the band gap energy, then the light will be absorbed and electron-hole pairs will be created.

As light goes through a material, its intensity will decrease. We can model this with:

I0 = the light intensity just inside the material at x = 0+

R-C Center Recombination / Generation

Let:

p = number of holes per cm3
n = number of electron carriers per cm3

Suppose that a semiconductor has an equilibrium concentration level of p0 holes and n0 electron carriers per cm3 . At time zero or before time zero we did something to the semiconductor so that it's concentration levels are not p0, n0 .

Gradually the semiconductor's concentration level will return to p0 , n0 .

Another way to view the gradual change is to define:

Δp = p - p0
Δn = n - n0

Δp and Δn would approach zero as time approach infinity.

Low Level Injection

This is a special case where the perturbation affects just the minority carrier.

If the semiconductor is n-type material, then low level injection means that Δp at its maximum is several order of magnitude smaller than n0 . During the whole transition, n ≈ n0 . However, Δp can be much larger than p0 .

If the semiconductor is p-type material, then low level injection means that Δn at its maximum is several order of magnitude smaller than p0 . During the whole transition, p ≈ p0 . However, Δn can be much larger than n0 .

R-G Center Generation - Recombination under Low Level Injection Conditions

Minority Carrier Lifetimes

If Δp > 0, it means that during the transition, we have excess positive carriers (holes) being gradually eliminated.

Tn and Tp are the average time an excess minority carrier will live in a sea of majority carriers.

The T's vary a great deal. The T's are dependent on the number of R-G centers, which is very poorly controlled. So the T's do not come from a chart or a graph, they need to be experimentally measured.

One idea is drawn below. The idea is to shine light on a semiconductor, creating excess carriers. Then turn off the light, and the excess carriers will gradually disappear through recombination. The changes in the carriers will effect the resistivity of the semiconductor, which will in turn affect the voltage read across the resistor RL .
