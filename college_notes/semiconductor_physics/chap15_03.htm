MESFET

General Introduction

MESFET = Metal Semiconductor FET

The idea behind the MESFET is that sometimes an MS contact can be like a diode, a rectifying contact that produces a depletion region.

A GaAs MESFET is pictured below.

Note that in these single gate structures, the width of the channel is "a", not the "2a" used in the ID equations for the J-FET.

These GaAs MESFET are used in high frequency applications where GaAs is preferred over Si because of its superior electron-transport properties.

There are two basic versions of MESFET. The enhancement mode version (E-MESFET) starts out with the gate-channel depletion already blocking out the entire channel. An forward bias is needed to reduce the depletion region and allow conduction.

The depletion mode version (D-MESFET) is the version in the previous discussions, where things start out such that the depletion region does not choke off the channel. A reverse bias VP is needed to block the channel and prevent conduction.

 

Short Channel Considerations

One thing already mentioned before is that the saturation of current at high drain-source voltages happen only if the channel is relatively long, such that the pinched off segment of the channel is a relatively short part of the overall channel.

Another effect of short channels is that short channels can lead to very high electric fields between the drain and the source. The channel in commercial devices is routinely ≤ 1μm. A 1 volt applied across such channels lead to average electric fields greater than 104 V / cm. The actual electric field is the greatest near the drain.

The ID equations are based on the equation for drift current, which assumes that higher electric field is directly proportional to electron speed. As seen from the following chart from chapter 3:

for high electric fields, the electron speed eventually saturates. So the electron mobility is high for small electric fields, but drops to zero for larger electric fields.

One way to compensate for short channel effects is to use a more complicated equation for vd(E) instead of just assuming that the electron velocity depends linearly on the electric field. The book mentions:

vd =
μ0 E
1 + μ0 E / vsat

The presence of an upper limit on the speed of the electron also means that the channel might not reach pinch off at all in some devices. The current Id is constant throughout all points in the channel. As the depletion region widens and the channel narrows, electrons must go faster to maintain a constant flow of current. where the depletion region stops growing at y = y1 . To model this case, break the channel into two different parts and use two different models. For 0 < y < y1 , use the previous long channel model where the electron speed vd = μn * E. For y1 < y < L, use vd = vsat . Also, in this case the saturation does not come from a pinch off in the channel, so VDsat is NOT VG - VP .
