Electrostatics

MOS = Metal - Oxide - Semiconductor. This designation is reserved for the common scenario of metal followed by SiO2 followed by Si. Other metal - insulator - semiconductor combinations are called MIS (Metal - Insulator - Semiconductor).

A picture of a MOS structure:

The silicon layer is also called the substrate.

It's assumed that the oxide layer, SiO2 , is a perfect insulator. Also, it's assumed that the semiconductor layer is sufficient thick so that a portion of it is free of electric fields --- this field free portion is called the Si "bulk".

Energy band diagrams:

The insulator band diagram is just like that for a semiconductor except that the band gap is much wider.

It's assumed that ΦM = ΦS so that the EF of both the metal and semiconductor are the same. The book says that this assumption is only temporary and will be eliminated by chapter 18.

In chapter 14, the band bending between metal  and semiconductor boundary comes from ΦM and ΦS being unequal, but here we are assuming that the two work functions are equal. The band banding comes from an externally applied voltage. If a voltage is applied across the MOS, a part of that voltage will fall over the starting portion of the Si --- resulting in electric field over a portion of Si and causing the band there to bend.

I think the reason the book said the "ΦM = ΦS" assumption can be omitted because if "ΦM ≠ ΦS", all that will do is create some default band bending. For now, we are assuming that the band bending is coming from an externally applied voltage.

Effect of an Applied Bias

The slope of the fermi energy EF remain flat because the SiO2 insulator means that there will be no current flowing through the metal or the semiconductor.

But the EF of the metal and the EF of the semiconductor will differ:

-qV = EF(metal) - EF(semiconductor)

Think of the two EF flat lines as handles. The semiconductor side is grounded, so the EF there doesn't move. On the metal side, the EF move upward if VG < 0 and downward if VG > 0. The reasoning is that VG > 0 means electrons being sucked away into the source, so the energy level of the highest electrons are lowered.

Within the oxide, the electric field is constant because there is no carrier. The EC and EV lines rise and fall in a constant manner in the oxide region.

Charge Build up due to Bias

Say the Si substrate is n-type. If a positive voltage is applied at the gate, then holes are pumped into the metal side. On the Si side the majority carriers, electrons, are created to balance out the excess holes on the metal side. The Si side therefore gets a negative charge and the metal side gets a positive charge.

The Si side has more majority carriers than the unbiased state, and this is called accumulation.

When VG < 0, electrons are pumped into the metal and electrons on the Si side of the oxide are pushed away. Those electrons will leave behind donors that look like positive charges.

The section of the Si side near the oxide now has less majority carrier than the zero bias state, and this is called depletion.

At the start of the depletion, it's just the absence of electrons --- holes are not really the majority carrier yet. As VG become more and more negative, more and more holes are created on the Si side. Eventually, the number of holes exceed the number of donors (pS > ND), where pS is surface charge desnity and ND is donor density. When the original majority carrier is somehow exceeded by the original minority carrier, it's called inversion.

VT is the threshold voltage that marks the onset of inversion.

When VG = VT , we are in the depletion - inversion transition point, where the number of holes equal the number of donors.

In this special state, we have:

Ei(surface) - Ei(bulk) = 2[EF - Ei(bulk)]

ΦS and ΦF are related:

ΦS = 2ΦF at the depletion-inversion transition point

So for a p-type semiconductor, ΦF > 0, and

  • 0 < ΦS < 2ΦF if semiconductor is depleted
  • ΦS > 2ΦF if semiconductor is inverted
  • ΦS < 0 if semiconductor is accumulated

For an n-type semiconductor the inequalities would be reversed because ΦF would be negative.

Accumulation solution:

In accumulation, the charges is approximated by a δ function at the boundary between the oxide and the semiconductor. The electric field (E) and electric potential (Φ) would both be zero for all x > 0.

Inversion solution:

In inversion, most of the charge is once again at the oxide-semiconductor boundary. Also, while the depletion width widens as depletion increases, this width only changes slightly once the semiconductor inverts.

The charge distribution solution for inversion is to add a δ function on top of the depletion scenario solution. The electric field and electric potential solutions for inversion are held fix at their ΦS = 2ΦF values.
