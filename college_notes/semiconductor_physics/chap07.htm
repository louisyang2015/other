Reverse-Bias Junction Capacitance

Small signal changes the width of the depletion region. The capacitance associated with the depletion width oscillation is known as the junction or depletion-layer capacitance (CJ).

The depletion region width oscillation is achieved by having carriers move rapidly into and out of the affected region in sync with the a.c. signal. If the carriers can indeed do this fast enough, then we say the device is following the a.c. signal quasistatically. The quasistatic assumption made here is valid up to very high signal frequencies.

Forward-Bias Diffusion Admittance

A comparison of forward and reverse bias models

Under reverse bias, the growth/shrinking of the depletion region is due almost entirely to majority carrier. That's because we have minority carrier depletion.

Under forward bias, minority carriers start to play a larger role. There is a build up of excess minority carriers near the depletion region. This build up will increase and decrease as the a.c. signal oscillates. But unlike the majority carriers, these minority carriers have problem moving in and out of the depletion region fast enough --- the minority carrier doesn't stay in sync with the a.c. signal. This out-of-phase charge oscillation enhances the observed conductance and reduces the observed capacitance. The capacitance and conductance associated with the minority carrier oscillation are expected to be frequency-dependent.
