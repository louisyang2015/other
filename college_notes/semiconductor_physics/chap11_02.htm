Deviations from the Ideal

Early Effect

The biggest deviation between theory and experiment can be found in the common emitter output characteristics:

Theory: Experiment:

The reason for increasing IC as VEC increase is called base width modulation or the Early Effect. As a larger and larger VEC reverse bias is applied, the depletion region at the BC junction grows, and the width (W) of the quasineutral base region decreases.

Under active mode biasing the EB junction experiences only small forward bias and plays a negligible role in modulating the quasineutral width of the base. The BC junction gets a much larger depletion region due to the reverse bias there.

By giving the base a higher doping than the collector, most of the depletion region at the CB junction will be inside the collector and not the base. This reduces the effect of based width modulation.

 

Punch-Through

If the reverse bias at the CB junction got sufficiently large, then it's possible that the entire base quasineutral region disappears due to the expanding CB depletion region.

In this case, holes injected at the emitter will easily cross into the collector region. Only a slight increase in -VCB gives rise to a large increase in IC . This is one way for the BJT to enter a breakdown state.

 

Avalanche Multiplication Breakdown

Just like the diode, a sufficiently large reverse bias at the BC junction can trigger an avalanche breakdown. For the BJT in active mode, there are two different scenarios for avalanche breakdown, depending on whether the BJT is in common base mode or in common emitter mode.

As the reverse bias across BC junction increase, more and more electron-hole pairs are generated by impact ionization. The generated electrons are send to the base. If the BJT is in common base configuration, the electrons can leave the BJT through the base terminal. If the BJT is in common emitter configuration, the base current is fixed, and the electrons can leave only by going out through the emitter terminal. This can result in a feedback because electrons entering the emitter from the base will lead to more holes going from emitter to the base, which then leads to a greater IE and more impact ionizations in the BC junction.

Book's diagram of the common emitter feedback situation:

The presence of the feed back means that common emitter configurations face a breakdown earlier than common base configurations. Although VEC ≈ -VCB , but VCE0 (for example 90 V) is much smaller than VCB0 (for example 120 V).

For BJT, this early avalanche due to feed back is detrimental. In other devices though this effect could be positive.

Recombination-Generation Current

Under most active bias conditions, the R-G current plays a small role. The bulk of the current going through the BC reverse bias is due to the holes coming from the emitter portion of the BJT.

 

Graded Base

Due to manufacturing methods, the doping within the base is not a constant as assumed in the ideal transistor analysis. A doping variation with position will give rise to a built-in electric field. If the BJT is made such that the field is assisting the transport of minority carriers across the quasineutral width of the base, then this deviation from the ideal would be beneficial.
