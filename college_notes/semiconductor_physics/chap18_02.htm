implies that if the ions are near the metal-oxide interface (x ≈ 0), the ∆VG will be small. If the ions are near the oxide-silicon interface (x ≈ xo), then ∆VG will be larger.

Experimentally, if we bias a MOS capacitor that has mobile ions in the oxide, the capacitance - voltage curve will gradually change with time.

This problem is usually fixed two ways. In phosphorus stabilization, phosphorus is diffused into the outer portion of the oxide. Under high temperature conditions, the sodium ions are highly mobile and will move to react with the phosphorus atoms.

We say the alkali ions are "gettered" or drawn out of the major portion of  the oxide. The ions are then fixed at a place where they give rise to the least amount of as-fabricated C-V curve shifting.

In chlorine neutralization, chlorine is introduced into the oxide during the growth of the SiO2 layer. Ionic sodium migrates into the vicinity of the oxide-silicon interface, becomes trapped, and is neutralized.

After MOS oxide layer shrink to less than 100 angstrom thick, the phosphorous stabilization procedure can no longer be used. Chlorine neutralization continues to be employed.

Eventually, a phosphosilicate glass layer is deposited on the IC via chemical vapor techniques. This layer helps to minimize ionic contamination subsequent to device fabrication.

 

The Fixed Charge

After the SiO2 is grown on top of silicon, there's a charge build up on the boundary between the oxide and the semiconductor. This charge is dependent on the procedure used to grow the SiO2 and is reproducible, so it's given the name fixed charge.

ΔVG ( fixed
charge 		) = -
QF
Co

where QF is the fixed charge per area and Co is the capacitance per area.

The fixed charge is independent of the oxide thickness, the semiconductor doping concentration, and the semiconductor doping type (n or p).

The fixed charge does depend on Si surface orientation and oxidation conditions.

The fixed charge is smallest for {100} orientations, and three times larger for {111} orientations.

Regardless of the oxidation condition, the fixed charge can always be reduced to a minimum by annealing in an inert atmosphere.

SiO2 comes from a reaction between Si and O2 . The fixed charge is always found on the boundary between Si and SiO2 , and this boundary is where the latest reaction occurred. It's been postulated that fixed charge comes from the excess ionic silicon that has broken away from the silicon proper and is waiting to react in the vicinity of the Si-SiO2 interface.

 

Interfacial Traps

Interfacial traps are extra allowed energy levels between the silicon and oxide. There are extra energy states introduced above Ec and below Ev as well, but those interfacial energy states are obscured by the large number of normal energy states that already exist there.

Donor like states are neutral when filled with an electron, and positive when empty.

Note that in the case of donor like interfacial traps, all levels under the fermi level becomes filled. The interfacial traps therefore introduce a charge that varies with bias.

The origin of interfacial traps may be the dangling (unsatisfied) bonds at the semiconductor - oxide boundary.

The density of interfacial traps is denoted DIT , with units of states / cm2-eV. The number of traps is constant over the  midgap region and increase rapidly as one approach the band edges.

States near the conduction band are believed to be acceptor like, while those near the valence band are believed to be donor like.

Like fixed charge, interfacial traps is greatest on the {111} Si surfaces and smallest on {100} surfaces.

Annealing in the presence of hydrogen at relatively low temperatures (≤500 oC) minimizes DIT . The hydrogen may come as water vapor, or it may be directly supplied to the semiconductor - oxide interface.

 

Radiation Effects

Radiation can create electron-hole pairs throughout the oxide.  Holes can eventually end up as trapped in the vicinity of the semiconductor - oxide interface.

The effect of fixed charges and interfacial traps is proportional to xo , the thickness of the oxide. As oxide becomes thinner, the effects QF and DIT reduces. Eventually, as oxide thickness drops below 100 angstrom, it might be possible that  electrons from the metal and semiconductor can tunnel into all parts of a very thin oxide and rapidly annihilate the trapped holes.

Negative-bias instability --- putting too large a negative bias over the MOS-C can work like radiation and eventually leads to holes that are trapped at the semiconductor - metal interface.
