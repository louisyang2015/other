I-V Characteristics

For small forward bias voltages, a dominant component of the p-n junction diode's current is due to recombination in the depletion region. Another way to look at this is that or a p-n junction diode, the potential barrier can not be lowered quickly enough to permit substantial current growth.

But for a MS diode, the barrier is smaller and the recombination current is not a dominant component even at low voltages. In other words, even at low voltages, the current in an MS diode grows so rapidly that the recombination current is unimportant.

Due to the absence of the RG current, the slope of the forward bias semilog plot is very close to q/kT. Also, the recombination current IR-G and the minority carrier current IDIFF are both very small portions of the total forward bias current. There is not much minority carrier current because the metal replaces the p segment, and unlike the p segment metals don't have many holes.

The reverse bias AC behavior essentially identical to that of an asymmetrically doped p-n junction diode.

The foward bias AC response of the MS diode is different from the p-n junction diode. In the p-n junction diode, it is the stored minority carriers that give rise to the diffusion admittance. The MS diode does not have this stored minority carrier, and so does not exhibit a diffusion capacitance or diffusion admittance. There is a forward bias depletion region capacitance, but this capacitance is frequency independent due to the lack of oscillating minority carriers.

Transient Response

MS diodes have very rapid transient response because is very little minority carrier injection and storage.

The MS diode can be used to speed up the BJT turn-off transient.

When the transistor enters the saturation mode, VBE > 0 and VBC > 0. The MS diode conducts and keeps the voltage VBC low. This keeps the charge build up on the BC junction low so that when the transistor goes from saturation to cutoff, the discharge will happen quickly.
