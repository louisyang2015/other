Carrier Drift

Carrier drift is when the carrier inside a semiconductor moves due to the presence of an electric field.

Heat also cause a lot of carrier motion, but this motion is random and is conceptually ignored.

Drift Current

Let say that we define a cross sectional area inside the semiconductor:

The number of holes that cross this yellow cross section per second is:

(holes per volume) * (area of the cross section) * (velocity of the holes)

Let:

IP|drift = drift current due to holes
q = elemental charge
p = holes per cm3
vd = speed of the holes
A = area of the cross section

IP|drift = qpvdA

Let:

JP|drift = density of drift current due to holes; drift current per area

JP|drift = qpvd

 

Mobility

Let:

E = electric field applied to the semiconductor
vd = speed of the carrier
μ = mobility

vd = μ * E

The exact relationship between the electric field and the velocity of the carrier can get quite involved. That's why the mobility number is used.

The unit for mobility is (cm2 / Volt-Second)

JP|drift = qμppE , current density due to hole carriers
JN|drift = qμnnE , current density due to electron carriers

Note that both of these density numbers describes a current in the direction of the holes. Standard physics define current as a flow of positive particles, even if the carriers are negative. Even JN|drift is describing the hole current that is caused by the electron carriers.

The more collisions a carrier moving through a semiconductor encounters, the less mobility will it be.

μ = q * <T> / m*

<T> --- the mean time between collisions
m* --- the conductivity effective mass

Lighter carriers (less mp* or mn*) has more mobility.

Higher temperature increase collisions and decrease mobility.

As the graphs above show, higher doping leads to lower mobility (more internal collisions). Note that mobility is not the same as resistance. Mobility is just how well can ONE carrier get through the semiconductor. Material resistance has to do with how much current can the semiconductor carry through. Although higher doping leads to lower mobility, there are so many more carriers that higher doping leads to less resistance.

 

Resistivity (ρ)

The units for resistivity is ohm*cm. To go from resistivity to resistance, multiply resistivity by the length of the conductor, and then divide by the cross sectional area of the conductor.

Let:

q = elemental charge
μp = mobility for holes in the material
μn = mobility for electrons in the material
p = number of holes per cm3
n = number of electrons per cm3

ρ = resistivity of the material

The book on pages 87 to page 89 talked about how resistivity is measured from wafers in real life. One way is to have four probes in a straight line across the wafer. Current goes from the first probe to the last probe, while the middle two probes reads the voltage. There are also machines for measuring resistivity that does not make contact with the wafer.
