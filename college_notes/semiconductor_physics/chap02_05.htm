Charge Neutrality Relationship

A semiconductor might have more holes than electrons, or more electrons than holes, but the net charge of a semiconductor is zero.

For each donor that is added to an intrinsic semiconductor, the donor's fifth electron break free to become a carrier. If the fifth electron does break free, then the donor will acquire a positive charge, and the free electron carrier counts as a negative charge.

For each acceptor that is added to an intrinsic semiconductor, the acceptor might get an electron to satisfy the octet rule. If the acceptor does get an electron, the acceptor acquires a negative charge. That electron has to be from some other atom, so the the atom that lost the electron to the acceptor gets a hole and acquires a positive charge.

Let:

ND+ = number of ionized donors per cm3

p = number of holes per cm3

ND+ + p = number of positive charges

 

NA- = number of ionized acceptors per cm3

n = number of electrons per cm3

n + NA- = the number of negative charges

 

ND+ + p = n + NA-

 

Let:

NA = number of acceptors per cm3
ND = number of donors per cm3

If we assume total ionization, then we can say:

p + ND = n + NA

 

Solving for n and p

For nondegenerate semiconductors facing total ionization, we can use:

np = (ni)2
p + ND = n + NA

to solve for n, p. Additionally, if NA is the dominant dopant such that NA >> ND and NA >> ni , we can assume p = NA . Alternatively if ND is the dominant, we then assume n = ND .

When the doping is sufficiently high, EF gets too close to EV or EC and the semiconductor is no longer nondegenerate.

Actually, this picture is a bit misleading. It turns out later, section 3.2, that under equilibrium, in a semiconductor that has different doping levels at different places, the Fermi Energy level is constant throughout the whole Semiconductor, and it's the Ei , EV , and EC that bends toward or away from the flat EF line.

 

How High Temperature Affects Carrier Concentration

As temperature goes up, more and more holes and electron become carriers. Through doping, you can have more holes than electron carriers, or more electron carriers than holes, but this difference can be overwhelmed by temperature. At high temperature, all semiconductor can become intrinsic.

GaAs has a higher band gap energy than Si. So more heat/energy is needed in the GaAs to create enough electrons and holes to blur the difference caused by doping. Therefore, GaAs devices can inherently operate at a higher maximum temperature than corresponding Si devices.
