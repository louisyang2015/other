Carrier Distributions

 

If there are X Si atoms, then there are 4X electron states total in the valence band, and 4X electron states total in the conduction band.

Density of States at a Given Energy

gc(E) =
(mn*) [2(mn*)(E - Ec)]1/2
π2 ħ3
gv(E) =
(mp*) [2(mp*)(Ev - E)]1/2
π2 ħ3

ħ = h / (2 * Π)
(mn*) , (mp*) are effective mass numbers from section 2.3

The units for gc(E) and gv(E) is 1/(cm3-eV)

gc(E) dE = number of conduction band states per cm3 lying in the energy between E and E + dE

 

Probability of a State at a Given Energy is Occupied by an Electron

f(E) =
1
1 + e^[(E - EF)/(kT)]

 

EF is the Fermi Energy, or Fermi Level. This number is depends on the material we are working on. This is a statistical function. By choosing the right EF , f(E) will work for any material, not just semiconductors.

T is temperature in Kelvin
k is Boltzmann
kT = 0.02585 eV

 

Important approximations for f(E):

If E ≥ EF + 3kT:

f(E) ≈ e^[-(E - EF)/(kT)]

If E ≤ EF - 3kT:

f(E) ≈ 1 - e^[(E - EF)/(kT)]

 

 

The Distribution of Carriers (at Equilibrium)

(number of states) * (probability of states being filled) = number of electrons present

gc(E) * f(E) = number of electrons present in the conduction band
gv(E) * [1 - f(E)] = number of HOLES present in the valence band

Most of the carriers are near the band edges --- once the maximum is hit, the distribution will decay very rapidly. It's the result of the energy band gap being much larger than 3kT, such that by the time we get to EV or EC , the probability is already close to zero and dropping fast.

For intrinsic semiconductors, EF is close to the middle. The presence of donors correlates to the EF going up, and the presence of acceptors correlates to the EF going down.
