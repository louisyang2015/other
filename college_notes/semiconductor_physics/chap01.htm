Semiconductors: A General Introduction

General Material Properties

The semiconductor family of materials include:

  • elemental semiconductors Si and Ge,
  • compound semiconductors such as GaAs and ZnSe,
  • and alloys like AlxGa1-xAs. The "x" is a number between 0 and 1. For example, Al0.3Ga0.7As would indicate a material with 3 Al and 7 Ga atoms per every 10 As atoms.

Si is far and away the most important. GaAs, exhibiting superior electron transport properties and special optical properties, is employed in a significant number of applications. The remaining semiconductors are utilized in "niche" applications.

Here's a table of the semiconductors. Except for the IV-VI compounds, all of the semiconductors listed are either elements from column IV of the periodic table, or are a combination of elements in the Periodic Table columns an equal distance to either side of Column IV.

Modern semiconductors are some of the purest solid materials in existence.

There are three classifications for the atomic level arrangement of solids: amorphous, polycrystalline, and crystalline.

The overwhelming number of semiconductor devices fabricated today employ crystalline semiconductors.

 

Unit Cells

A crystalline solid is an orderly array of atoms. The unit cell is a building block such that a large number of unit cells stacks together to form the crystal.

Miller indices describe what direction is a plane facing. For example, given the plane:

the intercepts are (1, 2, 3). We take the inverse of the intercepts and get (1, 1/2, 1/3). Then we scale this triplet so that all three numbers are whole numbers: (6, 3, 2).

The miller index for the plane shown above is (632)

Note that index relates directly to the equation of the plane: 6x + 3y + 2z = 6.

The normal of the plane would be (6, 3, 2)

If the plane is parallel to a coordinate axis, the intercept along that axis is taken to be ∞. When we invert it, the number would be zero.

If the intercept is negative, then we put a bar over the number in the final result. In the past example, if the plane's x-intercept is 1, then the miller index would be (632).

A number of planes would look the same due to crystal symmetry. For example, in a simple cubic unit cell, the (100), (010), (001), (100), (010), (001) planes are all equivalent. A set of equivalent planes is denoted {100}.

Miller indices can not be established for a planes that pass through the origin.

From Silicon to Wafer (Brief Introduction only)

Although Si is the second most abundant element in the Earth's crust, silicon never occurs alone in nature as an element. The silica obtained from the Earth has to go through elaborate steps to be purified into polycrystalline Si.

Next we go from polycrystalline Si to the wafers --- which are disks of crystalline Si. With the Czochralski method, the polycrystalline Si is melted. Then a small, crystalline Si, is dipped into the melt. The melted Si freezes onto the crystalline Si so that in the end we get a larger, cylindrically shaped crystalline Si --- called the ingot.
