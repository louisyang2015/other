The Ideal Diode Equation

Throughout this documentation, the left side of the junction is the p side, and the right side is the n side.

Carrier Motion in the PN Junction According to the Energy Band View

Note the triangle pattern. Electrons like low potential energy and their number increases exponentially with lower potential energy. Holes like high energy and they increase exponentially at higher energy levels.

The majority carrier currents are limited by the fact that crossing to the other side requires overcoming a potential energy barrier. The minority carriers can cross the depletion region easily, but there is limited number of them to conduct a current.

Under equilibrium, the majority carrier currents and the minority carrier currents balance each other out exactly so that carrier concentration remains the same.

If forward bias is applied to the diode, then the bending decreases, and the majority carrier current will out number the minority carrier currents. A linear change in voltage will create a linear change in the band bending, which in turn changes the number of majority carriers that can cross the depletion region by an exponential amount. So under forward bias conditions, a linear change in voltage leads to an exponential increase in current.

If reverse bias is applied to the diode, then the bending increases, and the minority carrier carrier current will out number the majority carrier current. However, the number of minority carriers is limited. So the reverse bias current quickly saturates.

Also note in the above pictures that under non-equilibrium conditions, the Fermi level is not constant through out the whole semiconductor. The Fermi level is split into two lines called EFn and EFp .

Under both forward and reverse bias, the current is maintained through R-G center generation.

The following pictures depict the scenario for reverse bias diodes.

First an electron hole pair is created on both sides of the junction via the R-G center process.

The minority carriers cross over the pn junction easily while the majority carrier is stuck.

The two additional majority carriers crowd out two similar carriers at the end of the diode.

The two carriers at the end of the diode interacts with the voltage source. The electrons go to the voltage source while the holes recombine with the electrons from the voltage source.

 

Minority Carrier Concentration in Quasineutral Regions

We refer to the regions outside of the depletion region as quasineutral regions.

In the quasineutral regions, the electric field is approximated to be zero.

Under forward bias, the overall picture for carrier concentrations:

The majority carriers are in green. On the left side, p = NA , while on the right side, n = ND .

It's not that the majority carrier concentration is the EXACTLY same everywhere, it's just that the majority carrier concentration is so many times bigger than the minority carrier concentration that the graph is flat.

The minority carriers are in orange. At places far away from the depletion region, the minority carriers follow the pn = ni2 rule.

Under forward bias, the majority carrier crossing the depletion region out numbers the minority carrier cross the depletion region. Once the majority carrier crossed over, it counts as a minority carrier. So there is a build up of minority carriers near the depletion region. This build up is highest on the border of the depletion region, and it decays exponentially.

If it's reverse bias, then minority carrier crossings out number majority carrier crossings. In that case there is a depletion of minority carriers. The carrier concentration graph for reverse bias looks like:

The majority carrier concentration is always a far larger number than minority carrier concentration, even for the forward bias case. So low-level injection conditions prevail.

Total Current

The expressions for minority carrier current gives something that looks like:

If we assume JN and JP to be constant in the depletion region:

we can add up JN and JP to find the total current density J

J = JN(-xp) + JP(-xn)

let:

A = cross-sectional area of the diode

I = AJ

Note that higher doping, NA or ND , would reduce current. I use to think that more carriers = more current, which is a misconception.

For p+ - n diodes, one can simplify I0 by using NA = ∞ and letting that term go to zero.

Likewise, for p - n+ diodes, use ND = ∞.

As a general rule, the heavily doped side of asymmetrical junction can be ignored in determining the electrical characteristics of the junction.

 

An Alternative Way of Getting the Ideal Diode Equation's I0

Exercise 6.4 on page 258 gives an alternative way to get the ideal diode equation's I0 .

Under reverse bias, there is a depletion of the minority carriers near the depletion region.

The np and pn drops off exponentially as they near the depletion region. Instead of dealing with the exponential drop off, we replace the exponential distribution with a square distribution extending over one decay length.

In the interval where np = 0 and pn = 0, minority carriers are constantly being generated. As soon as the carriers are generated, they are swept across the depletion region because under reverse bias, it's really easy for minority carriers to cross.
