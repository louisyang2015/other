Ideal MS Contacts

Here's a picture of a metal and a semiconductor separated:

ΦM is the metal work function while ΦS is the semiconductor work function. χ is called the electron affinity. When the metal and the semiconductor connects, the EFM, EC, and EV will move up or down so to have a constant fermi level energy (EFM) and a constant vacuum level energy (E0).

If ΦM > ΦS , it means the electrons in the semiconductor overall have a higher energy than the metal, and so electrons will flow from the semiconductor to the metal.

The lines EFM, EC, and EV rises by the same amount such that " ΦB = ΦM - χ " both before and after bringing the metal in contact with the semiconductor.

Electrons far away from the interface will have the pre-contact energy levels. The amount of bending of the EC band is to prevent those electrons far away from the interface from crossing into the metal.

amount of bending = ΦM - ΦS

The above picture is from the Kasap book, and that's why the notation is a bit different. A depleted region develops in the semiconductor that expands until it prevents the further transfer of electrons.

The ΦM > ΦS contact act as a diode.

Remember from section 3.1 that the voltage line is opposite to the energy lines (EC , Ei , EV ). Forward voltage has a voltage line that falls left to right, which produce a rise in the energy line left to right.

When forward bias is applied, the barrier ΦM - ΦS is lowered and a large current flows --- picture B. When reverse bias is applied, the barrier ΦM - ΦS is increased and only a small number of highly energetic electrons in the metal can cross into the semiconductor --- picture C.

The ΦM < ΦS contact act as an ohmic contact.

When forward voltage is applied, the EC line is raised, but there's no potential hill so electron can flow right to left unhindered. When reverse voltage is applied, the EC line is lowered, and the tiny barrier to electrons disappear so that electrons can flow from left to right.
