Deviations from the Ideal

The ideal region actually coves just a small part of the total graph --- interval #4. From left to right, the non-ideal effects are:

  1. Breakdown region, either avalanching or zener process.
  2. Additional reverse current due to thermal generation in the depletion region
  3. Additional forward current due to thermal recombination in the depletion region
  4. *** Ideal region ***
  5. Reduced slope due to high level injection
  6. Further reduction of slope due to internal resistance

Avalanching

In reverse bias, the minority carrier current is the dominant one. As the minority carriers cross the depletion region, they might collide with atoms in the depletion region. At some critical electric field ECR , the collisions are powerful enough that it frees electrons from the atoms in the depletion region. The newly created electron hole pairs are then swept away by the electric field, and they too collide with depletion region atoms and create more carriers.

The electric field is maximum at x = 0, so this ECR can be computed using methods of chapter 5. We get an expression for the electric field in terms of x, then we evaluate that expression at x = 0. For a step function, ECR is approximately:

Zener Process

This is when valence electrons on the p-side of the junction cross the depletion region using the quantum mechanic process of tunneling.

Normally, only high energy electrons (orange) can cross over the depletion region. But low energy electrons (green) can also cross over if (1) the depletion region width is very narrow, and (2) the bending is sufficiently great that there are empty electron states opposite of low energy electrons (green). To satisfy these two requirements, the Zener diodes are usually heavily doped on both sides of the junction.

The avalanching process can trigger a larger flow than the Zener process. However, avalanching requires a greater reverse bias voltage to work. The Zener process makes a significant contribution to the breakdown current where VBR < 6EG / q (~6.7 V in Si at 300 K), and dominates in diodes where VBR < 4EG / q (~4.5 V in Si at 300 K). Comparing this with the chart for break down voltage given earlier in this documentation, we can see that the Zener process dominates for diode with relatively low breakdown voltages.

Due to the special requirements for the zener process, avalanching is usually the dominant process. However historically, the Zener process was the first proposed to explain reverse-bias breakdown. All diodes that use this reverse-bias breakdown became known as Zener diodes --- even though many of them have VBR in excess of 6*EG /q and are functionally avalanche diodes.

The IR-G also does not saturate, because increase reverse bias widens the depletion region, which increases IR-G .

 

Thermal Recombination in the Depletion Region

Under forward bias, the majority carrier current is the dominant current. There are majority carriers that can not make it over the potential energy hill but still gets drawn in to the depletion region, where they recombine. Although these carriers do not actually cross the depletion region, from the view of the p side and n side, they went into the depletion region and did not return, so they count as current.

This current is also labeled IR-G , and the result for it is very complex. Furthermore, it does not matter much because under forward bias, IDIFF increases so quickly that it soon over takes IR-G . For the brief moment of time when IR-G is dominant under the forward bias, it makes the slope of VA versus ln(I) graph q/(2kT).

 

High Level Injection

One of the assumption during deriving the ideal diode equation is that the excess carriers at the edge of the depletion region is of low level injection. But when the forward bias voltage is sufficiently large, it's no longer low level injection.

The book didn't say much about the effect of high level injection, except that the high level injection effects are obscured by the slope-over associated with internal diode resistance (next item). In return though, high level injection can reduce the internal diode resistance.

 

Internal Resistance

A resistance can be assigned to the p side and n side of the pn junction diode.

Taking this internal resistance RS into account, the ideal diode equation can be rewritten as:

I = I0 e^[q(VA - I*RS)/(kT)]
