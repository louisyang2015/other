Cutoff Frequency

For a MOSFET, we define the input current iin as the current from gate to source. We define the output current iout as the current from drain to source. At low frequencies, the output current is larger because of the ( gm * vg ) amplification term. At high enough frequencies though, the input current can become larger than the output current.

If we short circuit the output (drain to source), the input current is:

iin = jw(Cgs + Cgd)vg

Cgs is the capacitance of the MOS capacitor CO mentioned earlier.

If Cgd is neglected,

iin = jw(CO)vg

The cutoff frequency is the frequency fmax where:

│iout / iin│ = 1

Small-Signal Characteristics

The gd is roughly a linear function of VG , going to zero when VG = VT .

The CO always has the low frequency behavior of the MOS transistor (V shaped, not L shaped), for even frequencies exceeding 1 MHz. The reason is that the drain and source islands are supplying the transistor channel with minority carriers so it can follow the AC fluctuations.
